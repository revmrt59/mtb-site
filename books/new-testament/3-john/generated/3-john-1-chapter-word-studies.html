Chapter 1: Word Studies

ELDER — (presbuteros, Strong’s G4245)

  1. Greek roots / derivation
    From the comparative form of a word meaning older, senior. Built on the idea of maturity in age, then applied to maturity in standing and responsibility.

  2. Meaning / definition
    An older man, an elder, one recognized as mature and qualified to lead or represent others. In the church, it becomes a recognized leadership role, but the root idea still points to maturity.

  3. Usage in Greek culture in Biblical times (with examples if relevant)
    Elders were community leaders who represented the people, handled disputes, and preserved communal order. The word carried weight because older age normally implied wisdom and proven character.

  4. Theological implications
    Spiritual authority is meant to be rooted in maturity and character. In Scripture, elder leadership is supposed to be shepherding, not controlling.

  5. Contextual nuance in the verse
    In 3 John 1, John calls himself “the elder” to speak as a spiritual father and trusted overseer. The tone is relational authority, not self-promotion.

  6. Key uses of the word in other verses (full NKJV text, transliteration after English word)
    1 Peter 5:1
    The elders (presbuterous) who are among you I exhort, I who am a fellow elder and a witness of the sufferings of Christ, and also a partaker of the glory that will be revealed. (NKJV)

Acts 20:17
From Miletus he sent to Ephesus and called for the elders (presbuterous) of the church. (NKJV)

  1. Other relevant insights or comments
    John’s self-identification sets the tone for the whole letter. Healthy authority starts with humility.

BELOVED — (agapetos, Strong’s G27)

  1. Greek roots / derivation
    From agapao, to love with covenant, self-giving love.

  2. Meaning / definition
    Dearly loved, cherished. Often used as a title of affection and honor.

  3. Usage in Greek culture in Biblical times (with examples if relevant)
    Used for close personal relationships. In Christian use it becomes a marker of family-level affection among believers.

  4. Theological implications
    Christian instruction is meant to be delivered inside love. The gospel creates a family, not merely an organization.

  5. Contextual nuance in the verse
    John repeatedly calls Gaius “beloved” because his goal is encouragement and strengthening, even when problems are addressed later.

  6. Key uses in other verses (full NKJV)
    Matthew 3:17
    And suddenly a voice came from heaven, saying, “This is My beloved (agapetos) Son, in whom I am well pleased.” (NKJV)

Romans 12:19
Beloved (agapetoi), do not avenge yourselves, but rather give place to wrath. (NKJV)

  1. Other insights
    “Beloved” is not sentimental filler. It frames the letter as shepherding.

TRUTH — (aletheia, Strong’s G225)

  1. Greek roots / derivation
    Built from a prefix meaning not plus a root connected to hiddenness or forgetfulness. Core idea: not hidden, not concealed.

  2. Meaning / definition
    Truth, reality, what is true as opposed to what is false or deceptive.

  3. Usage in Greek culture in Biblical times
    Often contrasted with appearance, illusion, or deception. In John’s writings, “truth” becomes deeply moral and relational.

  4. Theological implications
    Truth is not only information. It is a lived reality shaped by God’s character. In Johannine theology, truth is bound up with Christ.

  5. Contextual nuance in the verse
    “Whom I love in truth” means love governed by God’s reality and integrity. It is love that refuses hypocrisy and refuses lies.

  6. Key uses in other verses (full NKJV)
    John 14:6
    Jesus said to him, “I am the way, the truth (aletheia), and the life. No one comes to the Father except through Me.” (NKJV)

John 17:17
Sanctify them by Your truth (aletheia). Your word is truth. (NKJV)

  1. Other insights
    In 3 John, truth is measurable by conduct. Truth walks.

PROSPER — (euodoo, Strong’s G2137)

  1. Greek roots / derivation
    Eu (good, well) + hodos (way, road). Literally, to have a good journey or good road.

  2. Meaning / definition
    To prosper, to succeed, to have favorable progress in one’s affairs.

  3. Usage in Greek culture in Biblical times
    Used for travel success and by extension successful outcomes in business or plans.

  4. Theological implications
    Scripture recognizes that God can grant favorable outcomes, but it does not reduce blessing to money or comfort.

  5. Contextual nuance in the verse
    John prays that Gaius would “prosper in all things,” meaning his practical life would go well, consistent with his spiritual stability.

  6. Key uses in other verses (full NKJV)
    Romans 1:10
    Making request if, by some means, now at last I may find a way in the will of God to come to you. (NKJV)
    Note: the idea of being enabled to have a successful journey aligns with euodoo even where the exact English term varies.

1 Corinthians 16:2
On the first day of the week let each one of you lay something aside, storing up as he may prosper (euodootai), that there be no collections when I come. (NKJV)

  1. Other insights
    John sets prosperity under the authority of soul health. Inner order first, outer order second.

HEALTH — (hugiano, Strong’s G5198)

  1. Greek roots / derivation
    From a root meaning sound, whole. Source of English words related to hygiene.

  2. Meaning / definition
    To be healthy, to be sound. Can refer to physical health or soundness in teaching.

  3. Usage in Greek culture in Biblical times
    Used for bodily well-being and for the idea of something being “sound” or intact.

  4. Theological implications
    God cares about wholeness. Scripture can speak of bodily healing and also “sound doctrine,” both tied to the idea of what is healthy.

  5. Contextual nuance in the verse
    John prays for physical well-being for Gaius. It is pastoral, not materialistic.

  6. Key uses in other verses (full NKJV)
    Luke 5:31
    Jesus answered and said to them, “Those who are well (hugiainontes) have no need of a physician, but those who are sick.” (NKJV)

Titus 2:1
But as for you, speak the things which are proper for sound (hugiousa) doctrine. (NKJV)

  1. Other insights
    This word ties bodily health and doctrinal health to the same basic idea: soundness.

SOUL — (psuche, Strong’s G5590)

  1. Greek roots / derivation
    Related to breath and life. In common usage, the inner life that animates the person.

  2. Meaning / definition
    Soul, life, the inner self, the seat of desire, will, and personal identity.

  3. Usage in Greek culture in Biblical times
    Used broadly for life itself, and also for the inner person. Scripture does not treat “soul” as a disposable part of you. It is you on the inside.

  4. Theological implications
    God saves, sanctifies, and restores the inner person. The soul can flourish or wither.

  5. Contextual nuance in the verse
    John says Gaius’s soul is prospering. That means his inner life is strong, stable, and aligned with truth.

  6. Key uses in other verses (full NKJV)
    Matthew 16:26
    For what profit is it to a man if he gains the whole world, and loses his own soul (psuchen)? Or what will a man give in exchange for his soul? (NKJV)

3 John 2
Beloved, I pray that you may prosper in all things and be in health, just as your soul (psuche) prospers. (NKJV)

  1. Other insights
    John measures life from the inside out. That is a foundational biblical lens.

PROSPERS — (euodoo, Strong’s G2137)

  1. Greek roots / derivation
    Same as prosper above, good way, good progress.

  2. Meaning / definition
    Ongoing favorable progress.

  3. Usage in Greek culture
    Often used as a present or continuing condition.

  4. Theological implications
    Healthy spirituality is not a one-time event. It is sustained progress.

  5. Contextual nuance
    John assumes Gaius’s soul is actively thriving, not merely surviving.

  6. Key uses (full NKJV)
    1 Corinthians 16:2
    On the first day of the week let each one of you lay something aside, storing up as he may prosper (euodootai), that there be no collections when I come. (NKJV)

  7. Other insights
    John’s prayer is alignment: outward prosperity matching inward prosperity.

WALK — (peripateo, Strong’s G4043)

  1. Greek roots / derivation
    Peri (around) + pateo (to walk, step). To walk about. Figuratively, to live and conduct oneself.

  2. Meaning / definition
    To live, to behave, to order one’s life.

  3. Usage in Greek culture
    Common metaphor for lifestyle and conduct.

  4. Theological implications
    Christianity is a way of life, not a claim. Walking is obedience over time.

  5. Contextual nuance
    “Walk in the truth” means truth governs choices, relationships, speech, and priorities.

  6. Key uses (full NKJV)
    Ephesians 4:1
    I, therefore, the prisoner of the Lord, beseech you to walk (peripatesai) worthy of the calling with which you were called. (NKJV)

1 John 1:7
But if we walk (peripatomen) in the light as He is in the light, we have fellowship with one another, and the blood of Jesus Christ His Son cleanses us from all sin. (NKJV)

  1. Other insights
    In 3 John, walk is the evidence that “truth is in you.”

JOY — (chara, Strong’s G5479)

  1. Greek roots / derivation
    Related to charis, grace. Joy is often tied to grace as its source.

  2. Meaning / definition
    Joy, deep gladness, a settled delight.

  3. Usage in Greek culture
    Could be used for ordinary happiness, but in Scripture often becomes joy grounded in God.

  4. Theological implications
    Joy is not dependent on circumstances. It is rooted in God’s work and God’s faithfulness.

  5. Contextual nuance
    John’s greatest joy is not comfort, influence, or control. It is believers walking in truth.

  6. Key uses (full NKJV)
    John 15:11
    These things I have spoken to you, that My joy (chara) may remain in you, and that your joy may be full. (NKJV)

Galatians 5:22
But the fruit of the Spirit is love, joy (chara), peace, longsuffering, kindness, goodness, faithfulness. (NKJV)

  1. Other insights
    John’s joy is pastoral joy, the joy of seeing truth become life.

FAITHFULLY — (pistos, Strong’s G4103)

  1. Greek roots / derivation
    From peitho, to persuade, to be convinced. It moves toward trustworthy reliability.

  2. Meaning / definition
    Faithful, trustworthy, dependable. Can describe belief and also reliability.

  3. Usage in Greek culture
    Used for dependable persons and reliable commitments.

  4. Theological implications
    Faith is proven through faithfulness. Trust in God produces reliability in life.

  5. Contextual nuance
    Gaius acts faithfully in how he serves believers and strangers. His faith shows up as dependable action.

  6. Key uses (full NKJV)
    1 Corinthians 4:2
    Moreover it is required in stewards that one be found faithful (pistos). (NKJV)

Revelation 2:10
Be faithful (pistos) until death, and I will give you the crown of life. (NKJV)

  1. Other insights
    John praises reliability, not flash. That is a core discipleship value.

BORNE — (martureo, Strong’s G3140)

  1. Greek roots / derivation
    Related to martus, witness. Root idea is public attestation.

  2. Meaning / definition
    To bear witness, to testify, to speak as a witness.

  3. Usage in Greek culture
    Used in legal settings and public testimony. A witness is someone who speaks what they know.

  4. Theological implications
    Christianity is a testimony faith. God gives witnesses, and believers become witnesses.

  5. Contextual nuance
    People testify about Gaius’s love. His life creates credible witness in the community.

  6. Key uses (full NKJV)
    John 1:7
    This man came for a witness, to bear witness (marturesē) of the Light, that all through him might believe. (NKJV)

Acts 1:8
But you shall receive power when the Holy Spirit has come upon you, and you shall be witnesses to Me in Jerusalem, and in all Judea and Samaria, and to the end of the earth. (NKJV)

  1. Other insights
    In 3 John, witness is not merely preaching. It is reputation formed by integrity.

WITNESS — (marturia, Strong’s G3141)

  1. Greek roots / derivation
    From the same family as martureo, testimony language.

  2. Meaning / definition
    Testimony, witness, evidence given by a witness.

  3. Usage in Greek culture
    Formal testimony, evidence, affirmation.

  4. Theological implications
    The gospel comes with testimony: God’s testimony about His Son, and the church’s testimony about transformed lives.

  5. Contextual nuance
    The church hears testimony about Gaius’s love and truth-walk. This strengthens unity and discernment.

  6. Key uses (full NKJV)
    1 John 5:11
    And this is the testimony (marturia): that God has given us eternal life, and this life is in His Son. (NKJV)

  7. Other insights
    Testimony functions like spiritual evidence. It helps the church recognize what is real.

LOVE — (agape, Strong’s G26)

  1. Greek roots / derivation
    Related to agapao. In Scripture, defined by God’s action, not merely emotion.

  2. Meaning / definition
    Self-giving, covenant love that seeks the good of another.

  3. Usage in Greek culture
    Less common than other Greek words for love, but in Christian use it becomes central and richly defined.

  4. Theological implications
    God is love. Love is the mark of genuine discipleship.

  5. Contextual nuance
    Gaius’s love is expressed as hospitality and support. Love becomes visible action.

  6. Key uses (full NKJV)
    John 13:35
    By this all will know that you are My disciples, if you have love (agapēn) for one another. (NKJV)

1 Corinthians 13:4
Love (agapē) suffers long and is kind; love does not envy; love does not parade itself, is not puffed up. (NKJV)

  1. Other insights
    In 3 John, love supports truth. Love does not replace truth.

GENTILES — (ethnos, Strong’s G1484)

  1. Greek roots / derivation
    Ethnos refers to a people group, nation, a body of persons bound together.

  2. Meaning / definition
    Nations, Gentiles, those outside Israel in Jewish usage, often meaning non-believers in a given context.

  3. Usage in Greek culture
    Neutral term for nations. In Jewish and early Christian contexts, often used to distinguish those outside covenant community.

  4. Theological implications
    The gospel goes to the nations, but the church maintains integrity in how the message is supported and presented.

  5. Contextual nuance
    The traveling workers took nothing from the Gentiles, meaning they did not finance gospel mission by leaning on unbelievers.

  6. Key uses (full NKJV)
    Matthew 28:19
    Go therefore and make disciples of all the nations (ethnē), baptizing them in the name of the Father and of the Son and of the Holy Spirit. (NKJV)

Romans 1:16
For I am not ashamed of the gospel of Christ, for it is the power of God to salvation for everyone who believes, for the Jew first and also for the Greek. (NKJV)

  1. Other insights
    John’s point is about spiritual credibility. The mission should not be compromised by wrong dependencies.

FELLOW WORKERS — (sunergos, Strong’s G4904)

  1. Greek roots / derivation
    Sun (with) + ergon (work). Co-laborer.

  2. Meaning / definition
    A partner in labor, a co-worker in the work of God.

  3. Usage in Greek culture
    Common term for collaborators in a shared task.

  4. Theological implications
    God’s work is shared. Support is participation. Partnership is real ministry.

  5. Contextual nuance
    Those who receive and support faithful workers become fellow workers for the truth. Hospitality becomes ministry.

  6. Key uses (full NKJV)
    Philippians 4:3
    And I urge you also, true companion, help these women who labored with me in the gospel, with Clement also, and the rest of my fellow workers (sunergōn), whose names are in the Book of Life. (NKJV)

1 Corinthians 3:9
For we are God’s fellow workers (sunergoi); you are God’s field, you are God’s building. (NKJV)

  1. Other insights
    This destroys the false division between “front-line” ministry and “support” ministry.

PREEMINENCE — (philoproteuo, Strong’s G5383)

  1. Greek roots / derivation
    Phileo (to love) + proteuo (to be first). To love being first.

  2. Meaning / definition
    To desire to be first, to insist on primacy, to crave prominence.

  3. Usage in Greek culture
    The pursuit of honor-status was normal in the ancient world. John exposes it as spiritually dangerous.

  4. Theological implications
    Pride is a direct rival to Christ’s lordship. When a leader must be first, Christ is pushed aside.

  5. Contextual nuance
    Diotrephes loves preeminence among them. This explains his refusal to receive others and his harsh control.

  6. Key uses (full NKJV)
    Mark 9:35
    And He sat down, called the twelve, and said to them, “If anyone desires to be first, he shall be last of all and servant of all.” (NKJV)

  7. Other insights
    The letter shows how “being first” becomes practical harm, not merely an attitude.

RECEIVE — (epidechomai, Strong’s G1926)

  1. Greek roots / derivation
    Epi (upon) + dechomai (to receive, welcome). To receive favorably, to welcome.

  2. Meaning / definition
    To welcome, accept, receive with openness.

  3. Usage in Greek culture
    Welcoming guests was an honor-based social duty. In the church it becomes a spiritual practice.

  4. Theological implications
    Receiving the brethren is receiving Christ’s work. Refusing them can be refusing what God is doing.

  5. Contextual nuance
    Diotrephes does not receive apostolic representatives or faithful brethren. His refusal is a spiritual symptom.

  6. Key uses (full NKJV)
    Matthew 10:40
    He who receives (dechetai) you receives Me, and he who receives Me receives Him who sent Me. (NKJV)

  7. Other insights
    Hospitality and reception are spiritual discernment issues, not merely social manners.

DEEDS — (ergon, Strong’s G2041)

  1. Greek roots / derivation
    Basic term for work, action, task, deed.

  2. Meaning / definition
    Works, deeds, what someone does in practice.

  3. Usage in Greek culture
    Used for labor and outcomes, what a person produces.

  4. Theological implications
    Deeds reveal the heart. Salvation is by grace, but behavior exposes what rules a person.

  5. Contextual nuance
    John will “call to mind his deeds.” The issue is observable actions, not rumors.

  6. Key uses (full NKJV)
    James 2:17
    Thus also faith by itself, if it does not have works (erga), is dead. (NKJV)

Revelation 2:2
I know your works (erga), your labor, your patience, and that you cannot bear those who are evil. (NKJV)

  1. Other insights
    John is not nitpicking. He is dealing with patterns of destructive leadership behavior.

PRATING — (phluareo, Strong’s G5396)

  1. Greek roots / derivation
    Word carries the sense of bubbling over with empty talk.

  2. Meaning / definition
    To talk nonsense, to babble, to spread empty talk that causes harm.

  3. Usage in Greek culture
    Used for foolish speech, gossip, idle chatter, the kind of talk that inflates itself.

  4. Theological implications
    Words can become weapons. False speech is fellowship-breaking. Scripture treats destructive talk as a serious sin.

  5. Contextual nuance
    Diotrephes is not merely disagreeing. He is running his mouth in a way that damages people and undermines authority.

  6. Key uses (full NKJV)
    Proverbs 10:19
    In the multitude of words sin is not lacking, But he who restrains his lips is wise. (NKJV)

Titus 3:2
To speak evil of no one, to be peaceable, gentle, showing all humility to all men. (NKJV)

  1. Other insights
    Pride often expresses itself first in speech, then in control.

MALICIOUS — (poneros, Strong’s G4190)

  1. Greek roots / derivation
    Word can mean evil in a morally active, harmful sense.

  2. Meaning / definition
    Evil, wicked, harmful, morally corrupt, and often actively damaging.

  3. Usage in Greek culture
    Used for badness that is not passive. It harms.

  4. Theological implications
    Evil is not just “mistakes.” It can be a chosen posture that harms others and opposes God.

  5. Contextual nuance
    “Malicious words” are words with harmful intent, designed to tear down.

  6. Key uses (full NKJV)
    Matthew 6:13
    And do not lead us into temptation, But deliver us from the evil one (ponērou). For Yours is the kingdom and the power and the glory forever. Amen. (NKJV)

Ephesians 6:16
Above all, taking the shield of faith with which you will be able to quench all the fiery darts of the wicked one (ponērou). (NKJV)

  1. Other insights
    John is labeling behavior as morally serious, not just irritating.

EVIL — (kakos, Strong’s G2556)

  1. Greek roots / derivation
    Common word for bad, harmful, base. Often describes what is damaging in effect.

  2. Meaning / definition
    Evil, bad, harmful, what produces damage.

  3. Usage in Greek culture
    Used for moral badness and also for what is injurious.

  4. Theological implications
    Scripture treats evil as something to resist, not accommodate.

  5. Contextual nuance
    John warns not to imitate evil. He is calling for discernment in who we copy.

  6. Key uses (full NKJV)
    Romans 12:21
    Do not be overcome by evil (kakō), but overcome evil with good. (NKJV)

  7. Other insights
    “Do not imitate evil” means do not copy the methods, not just avoid the outcomes.

GOOD — (agathos, Strong’s G18)

  1. Greek roots / derivation
    Word for intrinsic goodness, beneficial goodness.

  2. Meaning / definition
    Good, upright, beneficial, what aligns with what is right.

  3. Usage in Greek culture
    Used for what is morally good and socially beneficial.

  4. Theological implications
    Goodness is a reflection of God’s character. It is not merely niceness. It is righteousness expressed in life.

  5. Contextual nuance
    John says the one who does good is of God. He is pointing to visible God-likeness.

  6. Key uses (full NKJV)
    James 1:17
    Every good (agathē) gift and every perfect gift is from above, and comes down from the Father of lights, with whom there is no variation or shadow of turning. (NKJV)

Romans 7:18
For I know that in me (that is, in my flesh) nothing good (agathon) dwells; for to will is present with me, but how to perform what is good I do not find. (NKJV)

  1. Other insights
    John’s “good” is not vague. It is goodness expressed in actions like hospitality, truth-walking, and humility.

TESTIMONY — (marturia, Strong’s G3141)

  1. Greek roots / derivation
    From the witness word-family (martus, martureo). Carries the idea of evidence given by a witness.

  2. Meaning / definition
    Testimony, witness, confirmed report.

  3. Usage in Greek culture
    Legal and public contexts. A testimony establishes what is credible.

  4. Theological implications
    Christianity rests on witness: God’s witness about His Son, apostolic witness, and the church’s witness in changed lives.

  5. Contextual nuance
    Demetrius has testimony from all, from the truth itself, and from apostolic confirmation. This is layered credibility.

  6. Key uses (full NKJV)
    John 19:35
    And he who has seen has testified, and his testimony (marturia) is true; and he knows that he is telling the truth, so that you may believe. (NKJV)

1 Timothy 3:7
Moreover he must have a good testimony (marturian) among those who are outside, lest he fall into reproach and the snare of the devil. (NKJV)

  1. Other insights
    Testimony is not hype. It is what a life proves over time.
