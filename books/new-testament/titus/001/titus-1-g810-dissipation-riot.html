Summary

DISSIPATION / RIOT — (asìtia, Strong—s G810)
Refers to a lifestyle of senseless waste, debauchery, and wild living that is "past saving." In Titus 1:6, it describes children whose conduct is characterized by an abandoned lack of self-control, rendering them a disgrace to their father's household. Blue Letter Bible

Full Study:

1. Greek Roots / Derivation
A compound word from a (negative) + sìzì (to save). It literally means "unsavableness" or "the state of one who cannot be saved." It describes a person who has spent all their resources (moral and physical) and has nothing left. Bible Hub

2. Meaning / Lexical Definition
Profligacy, wastefulness, or riotous living. It is the conduct of a person who is "out of control" and has abandoned all restraint. In the New Testament, it is the opposite of "sobriety" and "discipline." Thayer—s Greek Lexicon

3. Usage in Greek Culture & Word Pictures

  • The Prodigal's Spending: In Aristotle—s ethics, asìtia was used for a person who wasted their inheritance on temporary pleasures. The word picture is of a "leaking vessel" that cannot hold anything valuable because it is full of holes. Precept Austin

  • The Sinking Ship: In secular Greek literature, it was used for a shipwrecked state. It describes a person who has lost their "rudder" (self-control) and is being tossed about by every carnal impulse until they eventually sink.

  • The Abandoned City: It evokes the image of a city with broken walls, where there is no defense against invading vices. The person is "wide open" to every destructive influence.

4. Theological Implications
Asìtia is the ultimate expression of the "flesh" taking over the "spirit." It is the result of rejecting the "saving" (sìzì) power of God. While the elder is called to be "self-controlled," his children must not be "wild." A home marked by asìtia indicates that the "saving" influence of the father—s leadership has failed to take root, making him unfit to lead the church. BibleRef

5. Contextual Nuance in Titus 1:6
The NKJV says: "...not accused of dissipation (asìtia) or insubordination." In Crete, where the culture was "gluttonous" and "beastly" (v. 12), asìtia was the norm. Paul is demanding that the elder—s home be a moral oasis. If a man—s children are known for "riotous living" (drinking, partying, or wastefulness), that man has no moral standing to call the church to holiness. Enduring Word

6. Key Uses in Other Verses (NKJV)

  • Ephesians 5:18: "And do not be drunk with wine, in which is dissipation (asìtia); but be filled with the Spirit." NKJV

  • 1 Peter 4:4: "...that you do not run with them in the same flood of dissipation (asìtia), speaking evil of you."

  • Luke 15:13: "...and there [the Prodigal Son] wasted his possessions with prodigal (asìtìs—adverb form) living."

7. Other Relevant Insights
There is a direct link between asìtia and the "gluttony" of verse 12. Asìtia is the social consequence of gluttony—when you live for your "belly," your life becomes a "waste." The elder's children are the "test results" of his ability to cultivate a life that is "saved" from the cultural decay of Crete. Precept Austin
