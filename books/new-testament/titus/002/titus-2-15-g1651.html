Summary

REBUKE - (elenchō, Strong’s G1651)

Refers to the act of exposing, convicting, or bringing to light a fault with the intent of correction.

Full Study

Greek Roots / Derivation

A primary verb that carries the legal sense of cross-examining a witness to bring the truth to light. It is a tool of clarity, ensuring that "truth must be declared, not implied".

Meaning / Lexical Definition

To convict, show one his fault, or reprove. In the context of Titus 2, it is used to confront "lawless deeds" and behaviors that create an "integrity gap" between a believer's confession and their life.

Usage in Greek Culture & Word Pictures

  • The Bright Lamp: Used to describe bringing a hidden object into the light so its true condition can be seen and evaluated.

  • The Courtroom Proof: It carried the mental image of a prosecutor presenting undeniable evidence that leads to a change in the defendant's plea.

  • The Mental Image: Think of a leader who uses the Word to "straighten" a believer who has become "unstable" or "hardened" by cynicism or irritability.

Theological Implications

To rebuke (G1651) is an act of shepherding that protects the "spiritual health" of the community. It ensures that the church remains a "special people" by removing the "filthiness" that would otherwise discredit the message .

Contextual Nuance in Titus 2:15

The NKJV says: "Speak these things, exhort, and rebuke (elenchō) with all authority."

  • Paul emphasizes that Titus must not avoid areas of weakness; he must use the Word to strengthen and bring people to "maturity".

  • This rebuke is handled with "authority," meaning it is grounded in the "incorruptibility" of God's truth rather than personal opinion.

Key Uses in Other Verses (NKJV)

  • John 16:8: "And when He [the Holy Spirit] has come, He will convict (elenchō) the world of sin..."

  • Ephesians 5:11: "And have no fellowship with the unfruitful works of darkness, but rather expose (elenchō) them."

  • Titus 1:13: "...Therefore rebuke (elenchō) them sharply, that they may be sound in the faith."

Other Relevant Insights

  • Biblical Context: In Chapter 1, Paul used this word to describe silencing false teachers; in Chapter 2, it is used to correct the conduct of believers so they can "adorn the doctrine" .

  • Textual Application: Rebuking removes the "grounds for accusation" by ensuring that conduct and speech are consistent with the gospel .

  • Biblical Culture: In the honor-shame culture of Crete, a public or direct rebuke was a serious matter intended to move a person toward "sober-mindedness" and restore their honor within the "special people" of God.
