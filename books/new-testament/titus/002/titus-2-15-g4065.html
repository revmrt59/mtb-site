Summary

DESPISE — (periphroneō, Strong’s G4065)

  • Summary:: Refers to thinking around or beyond someone; to look down upon, disregard, or treat with contempt.

  • Functional Truth: A leader is "despised" when their life creates an "integrity gap" that allows others to bypass their words and disregard their authority .

Full Study

Greek Roots / Derivation

Derived from peri (around) and phroneō (to think or set the mind). It literally means "to think around" someone. It describes a mental process where an observer decides that a person's words are not worth hearing and therefore "thinks around" them as if they aren't there.

Meaning / Lexical Definition

To contemn, despise, or neglect. In the context of Titus 2, it refers to the danger of the church or opponents viewing Titus as insignificant because his life does not match his "sound speech" .

Usage in Greek Culture & Word Pictures

  • The Invisible Man: Used to describe a person who is so undervalued that they are treated as a ghost in the room; their opinions are bypassed in favor of others.

  • The Cracked Vessel: It carried the mental image of a container that is so visibly damaged it is deemed useless for holding precious liquid.

  • The Mental Image: Think of a leader who softens the truth to gain approval, only to find that people "despise" them because they no longer carry the "weight" of the Presence .

Theological Implications

To be despised (G4065) is the result of failing to be a "pattern of good works". When a leader's character is "incorruptible," people may disagree with them, but they cannot legitimately "think around" the truth they embody .

Contextual Nuance in Titus 2:15

The NKJV says: "Speak these things, exhort, and rebuke with all authority. Let no one despise (periphroneō) you."

  • Paul connects this to "all authority," showing that when a leader speaks God's Word, they must do so in a way that demands attention and respect.

  • The goal is to remove any "grounds for accusation," ensuring that the "word of God" remains the central focus .

Key Uses in Other Verses (NKJV)

  • 1 Timothy 4:12: "Let no one despise (kataphroneō) your youth..." (Note: A related but different Greek word).

  • 4 Maccabees 6:9 (LXX): Used to describe a martyr "despising" the pains of torture for the sake of the law.

  • 4 Maccabees 14:1 (LXX): Describes someone "despising" the emotions of the flesh.

Other Relevant Insights

  • Biblical Context: In the previous chapter, Paul exposed those who "subvert whole households" for "dishonest gain"; Titus must be the opposite so that no one has reason to "despise" his mission.

  • Textual Application: This command is the final safeguard for "healthy teaching"; if the teacher is dismissed, the "healthy lives" they seek to produce will never materialize .

  • Biblical Culture: In the honor-shame culture of Crete, to be "despised" was to lose all public influence; Paul tells Titus to maintain his honor through "integrity" and "reverence" so the gospel can spread.
