Summary

ENSLAVED - (douloō, Strong’s G1402) Refers to being brought into a state of bondage or subjection. In the context of "much wine," it describes a life where an appetite or substance has become the "master," preventing the believer from being fully yielded to the Lord.

Full Study:

1. Greek Roots / Derivation Derived from doulos, meaning a slave or bondservant. It carries the idea of being under the absolute authority and control of another.

2. Meaning / Lexical Definition To make a slave of, to reduce to bondage, or to bring into subjection. It describes a condition where one is no longer their own master but is compelled to serve the will of someone or something else.

3. Usage in Greek Culture & Word Pictures

  • The Captive of War: In secular Greek, douloō was used for a soldier who was captured in battle and forced into a life of service to his conqueror.

  • The Debt Slave: It described a person who had lost their freedom because they could not pay what they owed, resulting in their life being "owned" by another.

  • The Mental Image: Think of a person holding a leash; the one "enslaved" is the one on the end of the leash, forced to go wherever the master pulls.

4. Theological Implications The human heart is always serving a master. To be "enslaved" to anything other than Christ is to live in a state of spiritual "sickness." This word highlights that grace does not just forgive us; it changes who owns us. We are called to be "wholly His," which means we cannot allow any "other" master-including physical appetites-to sit on the throne of our lives.

5. Contextual Nuance in Titus 2:3 The NKJV says: “the older women likewise, that they be reverent in behavior, not slanderers, not given (douloō) to much wine...”. Paul uses the perfect passive participle here, suggesting a settled state of being "under the power" of wine. He warns that the older women must remain clear-headed and alert so they can fulfill their call to be "teachers of good things."

6. Key Uses in Other Verses (NKJV)

  • Romans 6:18: “And having been set free from sin, you became slaves (douloō) of righteousness.”

  • 1 Corinthians 9:19: “For though I am free from all men, I have made myself a servant (douloō) to all, that I might win the more;”

  • 2 Peter 2:19: “...for by whom a person is overcome, by him also he is brought into bondage (douloō).”

7. Other Relevant Insights In the "Mastering the Bible" framework, being douloō to wine is the opposite of being "sound." It creates a fog that obscures the "presence" of God. For the older woman, her life is to be a "revelation" of the Kingdom; if she is in bondage to a substance, her testimony is silenced and her "sacred fitness" (hieroprepēs) is compromised.
