Summary

REVERENT — (hieroprepēs, Strong’s G2412) Refers to that which is suited to a sacred person, place, or thing. It describes a life that is so "proper" for the Gospel that it carries the atmosphere of the temple into every ordinary area of life.

Full Study:

1. Greek Roots / Derivation Derived from hieros (sacred or holy) and prepō (to be fit or becoming). It literally means "suited to a sacred task".

2. Meaning / Lexical Definition Befitting a holy person; suited to a sacred character. It denotes behavior that is consistent with being a "priest" in God’s house.

3. Usage in Greek Culture & Word Pictures

  • The Temple Priestess: In secular Greek, hieroprepēs was used to describe the dignity and conduct required of those who served in a temple.

  • The Sacred Ornament: It described an object that was not merely functional but was "becoming" to a place of worship.

  • The Mental Image: Think of a person wearing "temple garments" in a common marketplace; their very appearance reminds everyone around them that they belong to another world.

4. Theological Implications Holiness is not just avoiding "bad" behavior; it is maintaining a "sacred fitness". This word reveals that the older women are to live as if they are always "on duty" in the temple of God, treating their daily lives as a continuous act of worship.

5. Contextual Nuance in Titus 2:3 The NKJV says: “the older women likewise, that they be reverent (hieroprepēs) in behavior…”. Paul uses this specific word to show that an older woman’s "behavior" (how she carries herself) should be a "matching piece" to her sacred identity in Christ.

6. Key Uses in Other Verses (NKJV)

  • This specific word (hieroprepēs) appears only here in the New Testament, emphasizing the unique, "priestly" dignity Paul expects from older women in the church.

7. Other Relevant Insights This term differs from the semnos used for older men. While semnos emphasizes dignity that commands respect, hieroprepēs emphasizes a "sacred beauty" that is fit for God’s presence. It suggests that their lives should be "life-giving revelation" rather than just moral performance.
