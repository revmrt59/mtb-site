Summary

ADMONISH — (sōphronizō, Strong’s G4994) Refers to the act of restoring someone to their senses, training them in self-control, or encouraging them to be "of a sound mind." It is a restorative process that brings clarity and "spiritual health" to the younger generation.

Full Study:

1. Greek Roots / Derivation Derived from sōphrōn (of sound mind, temperate), which comes from sōos (safe/sound) and phrēn (the mind). It is the verbal form that means "to make sound."

2. Meaning / Lexical Definition To recall to one's senses, to bring to one's right mind, or to train in self-control. It carries a strong sense of mentoring or "coaching" someone toward spiritual maturity and sobriety of thought.

3. Usage in Greek Culture & Word Pictures

  • The Physician’s Treatment: In secular Greek, sōphronizō was used for a treatment that restored a person from a state of delirium or "insanity" back to their "safe mind."

  • The Wise Counselor: It described the work of a mentor who helped a young person "curb" their wild impulses so they could live a disciplined, "proper" life.

  • The Mental Image: Think of a mirror being wiped clean of fog; the act of "admonishing" helps the younger person see the truth clearly so they can act with "sound judgment."

4. Theological Implications Holiness requires a "safe mind." Since the world often produces a "clouded" or "unhealthy" way of thinking, the body of Christ needs this "restorative" teaching. To sōphronizō is to participate in the "renewal of the mind," helping others to live as those who are "wholly His."

5. Contextual Nuance in Titus 2:4 The NKJV says: “that they [the older women] admonish (sōphronizō) the young women to love their husbands...”. Paul uses this word to show that "loving a husband" or "loving children" isn't just an emotion; it is a "sound-minded" discipline that often needs to be taught and modeled by those who are more seasoned in the faith.

6. Key Uses in Other Verses (NKJV)

  • This specific verb (sōphronizō) appears only here in the New Testament. However, its root sōphrōn is used throughout the Pastoral Epistles to describe the "sober-mindedness" required of leaders and believers (e.g., Titus 1:8, 2:2, 2:5).

7. Other Relevant Insights This term emphasizes the gentleness and wisdom of the older women. They are not merely "correcting" with a heavy hand; they are "restoring" the younger women to a place of "spiritual health." It is the "functional" application of "sound doctrine"—taking the truth and making it practical for the home.

8. References Blue Letter Bible: Strong's G4994 - sōphronizō; Thayer’s Greek Lexicon; Vine’s Expository Dictionary: Admonish, Sober.
