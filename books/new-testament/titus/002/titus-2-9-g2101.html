Summary

WELL-PLEASING - (euarestos, Strong’s G2101) Refers to that which is fully acceptable, gratifying, and delightful. It describes a life and service that goes beyond the "minimum requirement" to provide a "sound" and "fragrant" offering that brings genuine pleasure to the one being served.

Full Study:

1. Greek Roots / Derivation A compound word formed from eu (well/good) and arestos (pleasing/acceptable), which comes from areskō (to satisfy).

2. Meaning / Lexical Definition Fully agreeable, well-pleasing, or acceptable. It denotes a quality of service that is so "proper" and "excellent" that it leaves the recipient completely satisfied. It is the "functional" result of doing a task with a "good" heart.

3. Usage in Greek Culture & Word Pictures

  • The Perfect Offering: In ancient religious contexts, euarestos was used for a sacrifice that was "unblemished" and "fit" for the altar-it was exactly what the deity required.

  • The Satisfied Master: It described a servant who didn't just "do the job," but did it with such "internal hygiene" and skill that the master was "well-pleased" with the result.

  • The Mental Image: Think of a musician playing a piece of music with perfect "meaning integrity"-not a single note is out of place, and the performance brings a "sweet" delight to the listener.

4. Theological Implications The aim of the believer is to be "well-pleasing" to God. Since God is "infinitely good," our response should be a "sacred fitness" that seeks to delight Him in everything. This is not about earning favor, but about reflecting His character. A "well-pleasing" life is one that is "wholly His," where every "action" is a "spiritual revelation" of His grace.

5. Contextual Nuance in Titus 2:9 The NKJV says: “...to be obedient to their own masters, to be well-pleasing (euarestos) in all things...”. Paul instructs bondservants to aim for this high standard. By being "well-pleasing," the servant proves that the "sound doctrine" of Christ makes them the best, most "reputable" workers. This "proper" conduct makes the Gospel attractive to their masters.

6. Key Uses in Other Verses (NKJV)

  • Romans 12:1: “...present your bodies a living sacrifice, holy, acceptable (euarestos) to God...”

  • 2 Corinthians 5:9: “Therefore we make it our aim, whether present or absent, to be well-pleasing (euarestos) to Him.”

  • Hebrews 13:21: “...working in you what is well-pleasing (euarestos) in His sight, through Jesus Christ...”

7. Other Relevant Insights This word represents the "quality control" of the Christian life. It is the refusal to be "unhealthy" or "lukewarm" in our responsibilities. To be euarestos is to "adorn the doctrine" by ensuring our "spiritual container" is filled with "excellent" fruit. It ensures that the "Highway of Holiness" is a path of "sacred beauty" that others want to follow.
