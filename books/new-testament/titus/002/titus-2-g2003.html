Summary

AUTHORITY — (epitagô, Strong—s G2003)

Refers to a command, injunction, or a royal decree that carries the weight of a mandatory requirement.

Full Study

Greek Roots / Derivation

Derived from epitassì (epi meaning "upon" and tassì meaning "to arrange or appoint"). It describes an arrangement or order that is placed upon a situation by one in a position of command.

Meaning / Lexical Definition

An authoritative direction, a mandate, or a decree. In Titus 2, it signifies that the instructions for "sober-mindedness" and "reverent behavior" are non-negotiable standards for God's "special people" .

Usage in Greek Culture & Word Pictures

  • The Royal Injunction: Used for a decree issued by a king or high official that required immediate and total compliance from every citizen.

  • The Military Order: It carried the mental image of a commanding officer arranging his troops into a specific, disciplined formation to ensure victory.

  • The Mental Image: Think of a leader who handles the truth with "gravity" and "weight," refusing to soften the edges of the gospel to gain approval.

Theological Implications

To lead with authority (G2003) is to close the "integrity gap" by ensuring that the church is ordered according to God's design. It is the power that establishes "sound doctrine" as the immovable foundation for "healthy lives" .

Contextual Nuance in Titus 2:15

The NKJV says: "Speak these things, exhort, and rebuke with all authority (epitagô)."

  • Paul emphasizes that Titus—s mandate is "incorruptible" because it flows directly from the "blessed hope" and the authority of God our Savior.

  • This authority is what empowers a leader to address the "natural pressure points" of the body without fear of being "despised" .

Key Uses in Other Verses (NKJV)

  • Romans 16:26: "...according to the commandment (epitagô) of the everlasting God..."

  • 1 Corinthians 7:6: "But I say this as a concession, not as a commandment (epitagô)."

  • 1 Timothy 1:1: "Paul, an apostle of Jesus Christ, by the commandment (epitagô) of God our Savior..."

Other Relevant Insights

  • Biblical Context: This authority is the antidote to the "diseased" teaching of the "insubordinate" mentioned in Chapter 1 who were "subverting whole households" .

  • Textual Application: Titus must lead with a "weightiness" that makes the gospel believable, ensuring his conduct and speech are consistent with the "majesty" of the message .

  • Biblical Culture: In the Roman world, an epitagô was a legal command that could not be ignored; Paul uses this to show that the "Model for a Healthy Church" is a divine requirement for those waiting for Christ .
