Summary

PURIFY — (katharizì, Strong—s G2511)

  • Summary: Refers to the act of cleansing, making clean, or purging from defilement and filth.

  • Functional Truth: Purification is the divine "cleansing" of the heart that removes the "integrity gap" and prepares a "special people" to host the Presence .

Full Study

Greek Roots / Derivation

Derived from katharos (clean, pure, or unmixed). It describes the removal of any foreign or corrupting substance that would diminish the quality or health of the whole .

Meaning / Lexical Definition

To make clean from physical, ritual, or moral pollution. In the context of Titus 2, it refers to the inward transformation that produces "reverent behavior" and "sober-mindedness".

Usage in Greek Culture & Word Pictures

  • The Refiner—s Fire: Used to describe the process of heating precious metals until the dross (impurities) rises to the surface and is skimmed away, leaving only the pure gold or silver.

  • The Winnowing Fan: It carried the mental image of separating the wheat from the useless chaff, ensuring only that which is life-giving remains.

  • The Mental Image: Think of a home that has been marked by "chaos" or "rebellion" being thoroughly cleaned and ordered until it is a place where the "word of God" is honored .

Theological Implications

To be purified (G2511) means our character is being brought into alignment with the "soundness" of God—s Word. This cleansing is what enables older men to be "sound in faith" and younger women to be "chaste" and "discreet".

Contextual Nuance in Titus 2:14

The NKJV says: "...and purify (katharizì) for Himself His own special people, zealous for good works."

  • Paul shows that purification is the prerequisite for being "special"; we cannot be a "private treasure" while still clinging to "lawless deeds" .

  • This work is done "for Himself", indicating that our purity is for the Bridegroom—s pleasure and the "reputation of the gospel".

Key Uses in Other Verses (NKJV)

  • Matthew 8:3: "Then Jesus put out His hand and touched him, saying... 'Be cleansed (katharizì).' Immediately his leprosy was cleansed (katharizì)."

  • 2 Corinthians 7:1: "...let us cleanse (katharizì) ourselves from all filthiness of the flesh and spirit..."

  • 1 John 1:7: "...and the blood of Jesus Christ His Son cleanses (katharizì) us from all sin."

Other Relevant Insights

  • Biblical Context: In the Levitical law, katharizì was used for the ritual cleansing of lepers and the sanctuary; Paul elevates this to the moral and spiritual cleansing of the new covenant believer.

  • Textual Application: This purification addresses the "pressure points" of our natural weaknesses—such as "slander" or "impulsiveness"—and strengthens them until we are "sound" and "stable" .

  • Biblical Culture: Purity was a communal requirement for a people to remain in the Presence; Paul applies this to the local church, which must be a "pattern of good works" to the world.
