Summary

HOMEMAKERS — (oikouros, Strong—s G3626) Refers to one who stays at home and cares for household affairs. It describes a "sound" focus where the home is viewed not as a place of confinement, but as a primary sphere of ministry and a "spiritual container" where the Gospel is modeled and passed on.

Full Study:

1. Greek Roots / Derivation A compound word derived from oikos (a house or dwelling) and ouros (a guard or watcher; one who keeps). It literally means "house-guardian" or "home-watcher."

2. Meaning / Lexical Definition Staying at home; domestic; looking after household affairs. It carries the idea of being "busy at home," taking responsibility for the order and atmosphere of the domestic life.

3. Usage in Greek Culture & Word Pictures

  • The Guardian of the Hearth: In secular Greek, oikouros was used for a person who stayed behind to "guard" the home while others were away. It implied a vigilant protection of the family's resources and safety.

  • The Master of the Domain: It pictured someone who ensured the house functioned smoothly—making it a place of "sound" order rather than chaos.

  • The Mental Image: Think of a lighthouse keeper; their primary duty is to stay at the station to ensure the light never goes out, providing safety and direction for everyone in the "household."

4. Theological Implications The home is the first "mission field" of the believer. To be a "homemaker" in the biblical sense is to recognize that the domestic sphere is a "sacred fitness" (hieroprepôs) assignment. It is where "sound doctrine" is lived out in the most practical ways. It reveals that being "wholly His" includes the stewardship of one's immediate environment and the cultivation of a "reproducible" life of holiness within the family.

5. Contextual Nuance in Titus 2:5 The NKJV says: “to be discreet, chaste, homemakers (oikouros), good, obedient...”. Paul lists this among the traits the older women are to teach the young women. In a culture like Crete, where households could be "overturned" by false teaching (Titus 1:11), being a "house-guardian" was essential for protecting the family from spiritual and moral decay.

6. Key Uses in Other Verses (NKJV)

  • This specific word (oikouros) appears only here in the New Testament. Its singular use emphasizes the specific, focused role of the young woman in establishing a "sound" foundation for the church's future through the home.

7. Other Relevant Insights This term does not imply a lack of productivity, but a priority of presence. It is the "action" of ensuring the home reflects the "goodness of God." In the "Mastering the Bible" framework, the oikouros is the one who keeps the "East Gate" of the family, ensuring that only what is "proper" and "becoming" to the Gospel enters the domestic life.
