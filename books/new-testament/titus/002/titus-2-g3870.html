Summary

EXHORT — (parakaleō, Strong’s G3870)

Refers to the act of calling someone to one’s side to provide comfort, counsel, or urgent entreaty.

Full Study

Greek Roots / Derivation

Derived from para (beside) and kaleō (to call). It literally means "to call alongside." It describes a type of communication that is both close and purposeful, intended to strengthen the hearer for the journey ahead.

Meaning / Lexical Definition

To admonish, urge, or encourage. In the context of Titus 2, it carries weight as a serious urging rather than a casual suggestion. It is the process of training others in "practical godliness".

Usage in Greek Culture & Word Pictures

  • The Military Commander: Used to describe a general addressing his troops before a battle to instill courage and remind them of their duty.

  • The Legal Advocate: It was used for a person called to stand beside another in court to offer support and counsel.

  • The Mental Image: Think of a leader who refuses to let young men or workers "drift" by offering clear, direct instruction that calls them higher .

Theological Implications

To exhort (G3870) is a primary method for closing the "integrity gap" . It confronts the self-will of the believer and urges them to align their conduct with the "blessed hope" of Christ’s return.

Contextual Nuance in Titus 2:15

The NKJV says: "Speak these things, exhort (parakaleō), and rebuke with all authority."

  • Paul shows that exhortation is not optional; it is a mandate to ensure the gospel remains "visible" in every role within the church.

  • It addresses the "natural pressure points" of each group, using the Word of God to bring them to health and maturity .

Key Uses in Other Verses (NKJV)

  • Romans 12:1: "I beseech (parakaleō) you therefore, brethren, by the mercies of God..."

  • 2 Timothy 4:2: "Preach the word! ... Convince, rebuke, exhort (parakaleō), with all longsuffering and teaching."

  • Hebrews 3:13: "but exhort (parakaleō) one another daily... lest any of you be hardened through the deceitfulness of sin."

Other Relevant Insights

  • Biblical Context: The noun form of this word, paraklētos, is the title Jesus used for the Holy Spirit (the Comforter/Helper), highlighting that exhortation is a Spirit-empowered activity.

  • Textual Application: Titus is specifically told to exhort bondservants and young men, groups that require steady guidance to overcome "resentment" or "impulsiveness" .

  • Biblical Culture: In a society where many believers lived under authority they did not choose, exhortation was vital to help them "adorn the doctrine" through submission and excellence .
