Summary

LOVE THEIR HUSBANDS — (philandros, Strong’s G5362) Refers to a woman who is affectionate and devoted to her husband. It describes a "sound" and healthy affection that prioritizes the marriage relationship as a primary area where the Gospel is made visible.

Full Study:

1. Greek Roots / Derivation A compound word formed from philos (friend, dear, or warm affection) and anēr (man or husband).

2. Meaning / Lexical Definition Loving one’s husband; affectionate toward a husband. It carries the nuance of a warm, companionable love—the kind of love found in a deep friendship—applied specifically to the marriage bond.

3. Usage in Greek Culture & Word Pictures * The Harmonious Home: In secular Greek and ancient epitaphs, philandros was a term of high honor used to describe a wife whose devotion made the home a place of peace rather than strife.

  • The Inseparable Companion: It pictured a wife who was not just a domestic manager, but her husband's closest "friend" and ally.

  • The Mental Image: Think of two oxen yoked together who move in perfect synchronization; the philandros love ensures the "yoke" of marriage is not a burden but a partnership that moves forward with ease.

4. Theological Implications Marriage is a "spiritual revelation" of the relationship between Christ and the Church. To "love a husband" with philos affection is to demonstrate that the Gospel produces "sound" and healthy human relationships. It is a refusal to let the "sickness" of bitterness or indifference into the home, choosing instead to be "wholly His" by honoring the one God has joined you to.

5. Contextual Nuance in Titus 2:4 The NKJV says: “that they admonish the young women to love their husbands (philandros)...”. Paul includes this in the "training" for young women because, in the difficult culture of Crete, this kind of affectionate devotion was not always the "natural" response. It had to be taught and modeled as a "proper" fruit of sound doctrine.

6. Key Uses in Other Verses (NKJV) * This specific word (philandros) appears only here in the New Testament. Its companion word, philoteknos (loving children), immediately follows it, creating a "sound" foundation for the family.

7. Other Relevant Insights This term focuses on affection (philos) rather than just the volitional agape love mentioned earlier. It suggests that God cares about the "heart-connection" and the emotional health of a marriage. For the young woman, being philandros is a way to "adorn the doctrine of God" by making the Christian home an attractive witness to the world.

8. References Blue Letter Bible: Strong's G5362 - philandros; Thayer’s Greek Lexicon; Vine’s Expository Dictionary: Love.

how does LOVE THEIR HUSBANDS contrast with the greek word where Paul commands in Ephesians for husbands to love their wives

That is a sharp observation. The contrast between these two passages reveals a beautiful, "sound" symmetry in how the Gospel functions within a marriage.

In Ephesians 5:25, Paul uses the verb agapaō (the verb form of agape), whereas in Titus 2:4, he uses the compound word philandros (rooted in philos).

Here is how they contrast:

1. Source of the Love: Sacrifice vs. Affection

  • Husbands (agapaō): The command for husbands is rooted in sacrificial, volitional love. It is modeled after Christ’s love for the Church—a love that "gives itself up." It is a love of the will that acts for the highest good of the wife, regardless of how the husband "feels" in the moment.

  • Wives (philandros): The training for young wives is rooted in warm, companionable affection. While she is certainly called to agape her husband as a fellow believer, philandros emphasizes the friendship and the "heart-connection." It is a love of fondness that makes the home a place of warmth.

2. The Weight of the Command: Headship vs. Harmony

  • In Ephesians (Husbands): The husband is commanded to agapaō because he is the head. His love must be the "foundation" that provides security. It is a heavy, "priestly" responsibility to lay down his life.

  • In Titus (Wives): The young woman is "admonished" (trained) to be philandros because she is the "heart" of the home. Her affection provides the "atmosphere." Paul is emphasizing that a "sound" marriage isn't just a legal arrangement; it's a deep, healthy friendship.

3. "Proper" Fitness for the Gospel

In your MTB framework, this contrast is a perfect example of what is "proper":

  • It is "proper" for the one representing Christ’s headship to lead with sacrifice (agape).

  • It is "proper" for the one representing the Church’s response to be characterized by devoted affection (philos).
