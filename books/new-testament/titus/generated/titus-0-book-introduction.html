INTRODUCTION TO TITUS

A. Historical, Cultural, and Pastoral Context

The letter to Titus is written by the apostle Paul to a trusted co-laborer whom Paul left in Crete to complete unfinished work among newly established churches.

Paul states the purpose of the letter clearly.

Titus 1:5 For this reason I left you in Crete, that you should set in order the things that are lacking, and appoint elders in every city as I commanded you. (NKJV)

From the outset, Titus is concerned with order, leadership, church health and doctrine that promotes godliness and right living instead of engendering arguments or strife. The churches were immature and unhealthy. What was lacking was not enthusiasm or profession, but structure, leadership, maturity, and health.

1. Titus’s role and authority

Titus is not addressed as a local pastor acting independently, but as Paul’s apostolic delegate, exercising authority derived from Paul’s commission.

Titus 1:4 To Titus, a true son in our common faith: (NKJV)

The New Testament shows that Titus was a frequent and very close traveling companion with Paul. Titus appears in a variety of contexts and difficult situations. These required discretion, firmness, courage, and theological clarity (Galatians 2:1–3; 2 Corinthians 7:6–7). This indicates that Titus was seasoned, trusted, and capable of handling difficult pastoral situations. The instructions given in this letter therefore carry apostolic weight, not merely personal counsel.

2. Cultural context: Crete

It is likely that the churches in Crete began shortly after Pentecost, as Cretans were present in Jerusalem and may have carried the gospel back with them (Acts 2:11). The churches addressed in Titus were formed both within a culture that did not encourage moral discipline or truthfulness and in the absence of godly leadership. Paul describes the prevailing environment with unusual directness.

Titus 1:12–13 “Cretans are always liars, evil beasts, lazy gluttons.” This testimony is true. (NKJV)

Paul’s concern is not cultural commentary for its own sake, but pastoral realism. Instead of the church impacting culture, the culture impacted the church. This explains why Titus repeatedly emphasizes self-control, integrity, and conduct that visibly accords with the gospel (Titus 2:1–10; 3:1–2).

3. The internal problem facing the churches

Paul’s primary concern is the internal disorder and unhealthy influence within the churches.

Titus 1:10–11 For there are many insubordinate, both idle talkers and deceivers… whose mouths must be stopped… (NKJV)

False teaching, distorted motives, and lack of restraint were undermining households and weakening the church’s witness.

Titus 1:16 They profess to know God, but in works they deny Him… (NKJV)

This contrast between profession and practice frames much of the letter.

4. Sound (healthy) doctrine as the governing emphasis

Paul frames his ministry around truth that produces godliness. The purpose of truth is to promote godly living. Truth is not something only to believe, but rather something to be lived.

Titus 1:1 …the acknowledgment of the truth which accords with godliness. (NKJV)

Throughout the letter, Paul repeatedly stresses sound (healthy) doctrine.

  • Titus 1:9 holding fast the faithful word as he has been taught, that he may be able, by sound doctrine, both to exhort and convict those who contradict. (NKJV)

  • Titus 1:13 This testimony is true. Therefore rebuke them sharply, that they may be sound in the faith, (NKJV)

  • Titus 2:1 But as for you, speak the things which are proper for sound doctrine: (NKJV)

  • Titus 2:2 that the older men be sober, reverent, temperate, sound in faith, in love, in patience; (NKJV)

Healthy teaching results in healthy lives.

5. Leadership as the means of preservation

Paul begins with elders because leadership shapes the life and direction of the church.

Titus 1:7–9 For a bishop must be blameless… holding fast the faithful word as he has been taught… (NKJV)

Leaders are responsible first to live a godly life and secondly to ensure healthy teaching.

6. Grace and godly living

Grace is central in Titus, not merely as the basis of salvation, but as the enabling power for godly living.

Titus 2:11–12 For the grace of God that brings salvation has appeared to all men, teaching us that, denying ungodliness and worldly lusts, we should live soberly, righteously, and godly in the present age. (NKJV)

In Titus, grace does not compel obedience through pressure, nor does it merely forgive failure. Grace supplies what is necessary to live differently. What God commands, His grace provides. Godly living flows from grace received, not from self-effort imposed.

7. Good works as the visible fruit of faith

One of the clearest emphases in Titus is the repeated call to good works as the expected outcome of sound doctrine and genuine faith.

  • Titus 1:16 They profess to know God, but in works they deny Him, being abominable, disobedient, and disqualified for every good work. (NKJV)

  • Titus 2:7 in all things showing yourself to be a pattern of good works; in doctrine showing integrity, reverence, incorruptibility, (NKJV)

  • Titus 2:14 who gave Himself for us, that He might redeem us from every lawless deed and purify for Himself His own special people, zealous for good works. (NKJV)

  • Titus 3:8 This is a faithful saying, and these things I want you to affirm constantly, that those who have believed in God should be careful to maintain good works. These things are good and profitable to men. (NKJV)

  • Titus 3:14 And let our people also learn to maintain good works, to meet urgent needs, that they may not be unfruitful. (NKJV)

Good works are not the root of salvation, but they are its expected fruit and public evidence.

8. Context summary

Paul writes to Titus to establish order in the churches through sound doctrine, qualified leadership, and lives marked by good works. In a difficult cultural setting, the credibility of the gospel depends on a church that is spiritually healthy, doctrinally sound, and visibly transformed by the grace of God.

BIG IDEA OF TITUS

God’s grace establishes spiritually healthy churches through Biblical church government, sound doctrine, qualified leadership, and lives marked by good works.

CORE THEMES OF TITUS

1. Sound doctrine that produces godliness

In Titus, doctrine is never treated as abstract belief or intellectual agreement. Truth is given to be lived out, not merely affirmed. Paul repeatedly emphasizes sound, or healthy, doctrine because unhealthy teaching produces unhealthy lives. Sound doctrine promotes godliness, stability, and spiritual maturity rather than speculation, division, or strife.
(Titus 1:1; 1:9; 2:1–2)

2. Church leadership as essential to church health

Paul places immediate emphasis on appointing elders because Biblical church government is essential for the health of a local church. Qualified leaders protect the church from unhealthy teaching, model godly living, and provide stability in difficult cultural settings. Where there is a lack of Biblical church government or unqualified leadership, disorder and spiritual unhealthiness will follow.
(Titus 1:5–9)

3. Grace as the source of godly living

Grace in Titus is not presented merely as forgiveness, but as God’s provision for a changed life. What God commands, His grace supplies. Godly living flows from grace received, not from self-effort imposed. Grace enables believers to live soberly, righteously, and godly in the present age.
(Titus 2:11–14)

4. Good works as the visible fruit of faith

Good works appear repeatedly throughout Titus as the expected fruit of genuine faith and sound doctrine. They do not earn salvation, but they provide visible evidence of genuine faith and spiritual health.
(Titus 1:16; 2:7, 14; 3:8, 14)

5. The credibility of the gospel in a hostile culture

Titus is written in a setting where the surrounding culture did not reinforce truth or godliness. Paul’s concern is that the gospel not be dishonored. Lives marked by self-control, integrity, humility, and good works serve to commend the gospel and protect its public witness.
(Titus 2:5, 8, 10; 3:1–2)

6. Order as a servant of spiritual life

Paul’s call to “set in order” what was lacking reflects a concern for structure that supports life, not control for its own sake. Biblical church government, healthy teaching, and godly leadership, creates an environment where spiritual growth and maturity can flourish and make for a healthy local church.
(Titus 1:5; 3:1)
