COMMANDMENT — (epitagô, StrongΓÇÖs G2003)
Refers to an authoritative directive or a royal injunction that demands immediate compliance. In Titus 1:3, it emphasizes that PaulΓÇÖs ministry was not a personal career choice but a divine mandate issued by the Sovereign God, leaving him with no option but to obey. Blue Letter Bible

Full Study:

1. Greek Roots / Derivation
Derived from epitassì, which comes from epi (upon/over) + tassì (to arrange or appoint). It literally means "an appointment placed upon someone." Precept Austin

2. Meaning / Lexical Definition
An injunction, mandate, or decree. It denotes an official order that carries "authoritativeness." Unlike a suggestion or advice, an epitagô is a legal requirement that binds the recipient to a specific course of action. ThayerΓÇÖs Greek Lexicon

3. Usage in Greek Culture & Word Pictures

  • The Royal Edict: In secular Greek life, epitagô was used for a kingΓÇÖs formal decree or a government's official mandate. It was the highest form of communication from a ruler to their subjects.

  • The Military Deployment: It was a military term for placing a soldier in their proper rank or station. The word picture is of a General "ordering" a soldier to a specific post; the soldier does not negotiate his position but occupies it because of the epitagô. Precept Austin

  • The Irresistible Force: It evokes the image of a formal commission given to a diplomat. The diplomat does not speak for himself; he is "under orders" (epitagô) and must represent his governmentΓÇÖs interests perfectly.

4. Theological Implications
GodΓÇÖs commandment is the source of all legitimate spiritual authority. PaulΓÇÖs mission was not based on human ambition but on divine compulsion. An epitagô from God is both an honor and a heavy responsibility—it means the "steward" (v. 3) is not free to innovate or abandon the message. It underscores that the Gospel is a royal edict from the King of Kings. BibleRef

5. Contextual Nuance in Titus 1:3
The NKJV says: "...which was committed to me according to the commandment (epitagô) of God our Savior." Paul is answering the "Why?" of his life. He preaches because he was ordered to do so by the highest possible Authority. In the "insubordinate" (v. 10) culture of Crete, PaulΓÇÖs authority to appoint elders (v. 5) rested on the fact that he was acting under a direct epitagô from God Himself. Precept Austin

6. Key Uses in Other Verses (NKJV)

  • 1 Timothy 1:1: "Paul, an apostle of Jesus Christ, by the commandment (epitagô) of God our Savior..." NKJV

  • Romans 16:26: "...according to the commandment (epitagô) of the everlasting God, made known to all nations..."

  • Titus 2:15: "Speak these things, exhort, and rebuke with all authority (epitagô). Let no one despise you."

7. Other Relevant Insights
There is a profound contrast between the epitagô (God's order) and the "commandments of men" (entolô) in verse 14. PaulΓÇÖs authority is rooted in a sovereign decree, while the false teachersΓÇÖ authority is rooted in human fables. One leads to godliness; the other leads to the "upsetting of households" (v. 11).
