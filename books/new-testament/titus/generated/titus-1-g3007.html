LACKING — (leipì, StrongΓÇÖs G3007)
Refers to things that are left behind, unfinished, or absent from their proper place. In Titus 1:5, it identifies the "gaps" in the churchΓÇÖs structural and moral foundation that prevented it from being complete and healthy.

Full Study:

1. Greek Roots / Derivation
A primary verb (leipì) meaning to leave, to forsake, or to be destitute. It describes a "deficit" or a "falling short" of a required standard.

2. Meaning / Lexical Definition
To leave behind; to be wanting or to fail. In the New Testament, it is often used for a lack of resources or a lack of maturity. In the context of Titus, it refers specifically to the organizational and leadership deficiencies in the Cretan churches.

Precept Austin +3

3. Usage in Greek Culture & Word Pictures

  • The Unfinished Journey: In secular Greek, leipì was used for a traveler who abandoned the road or fell behind the rest of the group. The church in Crete was "falling behind" because it lacked the leadership to keep it on the right path.

  • The Defective Machine: It described a mechanism where a crucial part was missing. A clock without a gear or a ship without a rudder is leipì—it exists, but it cannot function.

  • The Rear Guard: In military terms, it referred to troops left behind or "missing in action." Paul had moved on, but there were vital "rear guard" elements of church order that were still missing.

Precept Austin +4

4. Theological Implications
GodΓÇÖs desire for His people is wholeness (teleios). When things are "lacking," the church is vulnerable to the "tornado" of false teaching (v. 10). Leipì reminds us that a church is not fully "set in order" just because people have believed the Gospel; it must also have the "missing pieces" of leadership and discipline to protect that faith.

Christian Classics Ethereal Library +3

5. Contextual Nuance in Titus 1:5
The NKJV says: "...that you should set in order the things that are lacking (leipì)..." The primary "lack" Paul has in mind is the plurality of elders. Without godly overseers, the "crooked" culture of Crete (v. 12) was infiltrating the church. TitusΓÇÖs job was to "fill the gap" by identifying and appointing qualified men.

6. Key Uses in Other Verses (NKJV)

  • James 1:4: "...that you may be perfect and complete, lacking (leipì) nothing."

  • James 1:5: "If any of you lacks (leipì) wisdom, let him ask of God..."

  • Luke 18:22: "You still lack (leipì) one thing. Sell all that you have..."

7. Other Relevant Insights
There is a link between leipì and the "unfinished" nature of a young church. Paul shows great humility by allowing Titus to finish the work he started. It suggests that ministry is a relay race; one person may plant, but another must come to provide the "missing" structure.
