Summary

STEWARD — (oikonomos, StrongΓÇÖs G3623)
Refers to a manager of a household or estate who handles affairs on behalf of the owner. In Titus 1:7, it identifies the leader as one who does not own the church but is legally and spiritually responsible to God for how he manages His "household" and resources. Blue Letter Bible

Full Study:

1. Greek Roots / Derivation
A compound word from oikos (house) + nemì (to dispense or manage). It literally means "house-distributor" or "manager of the household." Bible Hub

2. Meaning / Lexical Definition
The manager of a household or of household affairs; a superintendent. It describes a person who has been granted authority by a master to oversee his property, finances, and other servants. The steward owns nothing but manages everything. ThayerΓÇÖs Greek Lexicon

3. Usage in Greek Culture & Word Pictures

  • The Trusted Slave: In Greco-Roman society, the oikonomos was often a highly educated slave or freedman. He held the keys to the granary and the treasury. The word picture is of a man carrying a heavy ring of keys—he has access to all the master's wealth, but only to distribute it according to the master's will. Precept Austin

  • The ShipΓÇÖs Purser: It was used for the official on a ship responsible for the provisions and cargo. If he was unfaithful, the crew would starve or the owner would go bankrupt.

  • The City Treasurer: In civic life (like Erastus in Romans 16:23), it was a title for a high-ranking official who managed the cityΓÇÖs public funds.

4. Theological Implications
Leadership is a sacred trust, not a personal possession. The "Bishop" (Overseer) is a "Steward of God." This implies that the church belongs to God, the message belongs to God, and the people belong to God. The leaderΓÇÖs primary virtue must be faithfulness (1 Cor 4:2). He is an "accountable agent" who will one day have to "give an account of his stewardship" (Luke 16:2). BibleRef

5. Contextual Nuance in Titus 1:7
The NKJV says: "...as a steward (oikonomos) of God..." Paul uses this to explain why the qualifications are so high. You wouldn't hire a "self-willed" or "greedy" man (v. 7) to manage your bank account; why would God hire such a man to manage His church? In Crete, where people were "lazy gluttons" (v. 12), a "Steward" stood out as someone who lived for the Master's profit, not his own "dishonest gain" (v. 11). Enduring Word

6. Key Uses in Other Verses (NKJV)

  • 1 Corinthians 4:1: "Let a man so consider us, as servants of Christ and stewards (oikonomos) of the mysteries of God." NKJV

  • 1 Peter 4:10: "...as good stewards (oikonomos) of the manifold grace of God."

  • Luke 12:42: "And the Lord said, 'Who then is that faithful and wise steward (oikonomos)...?'"

7. Other Relevant Insights
The oikonomos must be "blameless." If the manager is corrupt, the entire household suffers. Paul insists that the elder's domestic management (v. 6) is the "resume" for his divine stewardship. If he cannot manage his own oikos (house), he is disqualified from being an oikonomos of God's house. Precept Austin
