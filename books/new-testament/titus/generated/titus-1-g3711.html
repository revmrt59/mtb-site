Summary

QUICK-TEMPERED — (orgilos, StrongΓÇÖs G3711)
Refers to a person who is prone to anger or easily provoked to a state of settled resentment. In Titus 1:7, it describes a temperament that is "combustible," rendering a man unfit for leadership because his judgment is constantly clouded by impulsive or lingering wrath. Blue Letter Bible

Full Study:

1. Greek Roots / Derivation
Derived from orgô, which refers to a settled, abiding anger or "wrath." It is a state of mind that has "ripened" into indignation. Bible Hub

2. Meaning / Lexical Definition
Irascible, prone to anger, or passionate. It does not just describe a single outburst, but a disposition toward anger. It identifies a man who is "ready to fly into a passion" at the slightest provocation. ThayerΓÇÖs Greek Lexicon

3. Usage in Greek Culture & Word Pictures

  • The Smoldering Fire: Aristotle used orgilos for a man who nursed his anger and was difficult to appease. The word picture is of a slow-burning fire that produces toxic smoke and can flare up into a blaze at any moment. Precept Austin

  • The Fermenting Must: It evokes the image of fermenting wine (must) that is bubbling and building pressure. If there is no vent for the "gas," the vessel will eventually explode.

  • The Trigger-Happy Soldier: In military terms, it described someone with a "quick thumb" on their weapon—someone whose lack of restraint made them a danger to their own unit during a tense standoff.

4. Theological Implications
"The wrath of man does not produce the righteousness of God" (James 1:20). An elder must represent the "God of all patience." If a leader is orgilos, he uses fear and intimidation to lead rather than love and truth. Anger is often the result of "self-will" (authadôs) being challenged; when we don't get our way, we get angry. A "quick-tempered" man cannot be a "steward of God" because he is a slave to his own emotions. BibleRef

5. Contextual Nuance in Titus 1:7
The NKJV says: "...not self-willed, not quick-tempered (orgilos)..." Paul is building a profile of self-control. In the "beastly" culture of Crete (v. 12), violence and hot-headedness were common. An elderΓÇÖs calmness was his greatest credential. If he was quick-tempered, he would "upset households" (v. 11) with his tongue just as easily as the false teachers did with their fables. Enduring Word

6. Key Uses in Other Verses (NKJV)

  • Note: This specific adjective is used only once in the NT (Titus 1:7). However, its root orgô is frequent:

  • James 1:19: "...let every man be swift to hear, slow to speak, slow to wrath (orgô)." NKJV

  • Proverbs 22:24 (LXX): "Make no friendship with an angry (orgilos) man..."

  • Colossians 3:8: "But now you yourselves are to put off all these: anger (orgô), wrath, malice..."

7. Other Relevant Insights
There is a link between orgilos and the ability to "refute" (v. 9). To correct a false teacher, one must have "cool" logic and "hot" truth. If the elder gets "hot" (angry), he loses his ability to persuade. "A soft answer turns away wrath," but a orgilos leader only adds fuel to the fire. Precept Austin
