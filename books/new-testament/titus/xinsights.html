Insight: Healthy Teaching vs Bible Teaching

Paul differtiates between “sound” or healthy teaching vs teaching from the Bible. The Judiazers and false teachers were teaching from the Bible but because the teaching was not heathy, the fruit of the teaching, meaning the teachers and the believers was not healthy.

This raises a radical point. You can teach something found in the Bible, but it not be a healthy teaching. You can teach things from the word of God and be an unhealthy teacher.

The question becomes what constitutes “healthy teaching”. I believe Paul answers that first to Timothy and then to Titus.

The Word of God must be used to teach the purpose for which God gave His word.

1 Timothy 1:5 The purpose of my instruction is that all believers would be filled with love that comes from a pure heart, a clear conscience, and genuine faith. (NLT-SE)

Heathy teaching does five things:

  1. Causes us to love Jesus more

  2. Cuases us to love others more, including our enemies

  3. Purifies our inner mean to be like Jesus

  4. Guides us to live a life pleasing to God in all circumstances, living like Jesus, suffering like Jesus, loving like Jesus.

  5. Causes us to have a steadfast trust in God. (Faith is believing who God has declared Himself to be in His Word.

If the word is used to promote arguments, strife, defend non-essential doctrines, or other-wise divide believers, in my view it is missing the purpose for which God gave us His Word.

Insight: The Life That Makes the Gospel Beautiful

The truth about Christ is adorned when believers live differently under pressure.

Titus 2 shows that the gospel becomes attractive when it produces an unselfish, submitted, cheerful, faithful, and life of integrity, especially in places where we are not treated fairly, feel constrained, overlooked, mistreated, or convinced we deserve better.

A life that adorns the Gospel isΓÇª

  1. An unselfish life dies to self and seeks the good of others first. In environments where selfishness feels justified, grace teaches us to serve.

  2. A submitted life willingly places itself under legitimate authority, not because authority is perfect, but because Christ is Lord. It reflects inward surrender, not forced compliance.

  3. A cheerful life goes beyond bare minimum obedience. It refuses murmuring, complaining, resentment, and self-pity. It remembers that God is in control and that we ultimately serve Him. It remembers our reward is from Him.

  4. A life of integrity lives under the gaze of God. It is the same at home, at work, and in church. It refuses small compromises, even when no one is watching.

  5. A faithful life remains steady in ordinary and pressured environments. It does not only shine when circumstances are favorable. It does not breakdown when things are unfair. It keeps showing up. It keeps doing what is right. It keeps honoring Christ when no one applauds. Faithfulness is consistency over time. It is reliability when no one is watching. It is obedience when obedience is costly. It is staying true when shortcuts would be easier. Anyone can say they believe. Faithfulness demonstrates it.

The message of salvation must show up in the most ordinary and difficult parts of life. That is where the Gospel becomes undeniable. Every facet of live becomes a stage where the character of Christ is either displayed or denied.

Insight: Six Marks of Spiritual Maturity

In Titus 2:2, Paul lists six qualities that should characterize these men. These aren't just personality traits; they are the results of sound doctrine incorporated into a life.

The list can be divided into two categories: Internal Restraint and External Faith.

1. Internal Restraint (Temperament)

  • Sober-minded: This literally refers to being "temperate" or moderate in the use of wine, but it carries the broader meaning of being clear-headed. An older man should not be controlled by his physical appetites. They should be clear-headed, not ruled by appetite or impulse.

  • Dignified: This means being worthy of respect. It doesn—t mean being "stiff" or "humorless," but rather having a serious-mindedness about life and faith that commands the respect of the younger generation. There is weight to their life, having walked decades with the Lord.

  • Self-controlled: This is a key word throughout Titus. It refers to a person who has mastery over their desires, impulses, thoughts, reactions, and speech.

2. External Faith (The "Triple Threat" of Health)

Paul concludes the verse by stating that these men must be "sound in faith, in love, and in steadfastness." Note that he uses the word "sound" (healthy) again here.

  • Sound in Faith: Their trust in God must be firm and free from the "sickness" of doubt or false teaching. They have unwaivering trust in God, regardless of trials or circumstances. Still bearing fruit in later years. No smell of “smoke— from going through life.

  • Sound in Love: As men age, there is a natural tendency to become cynical or self-absorbed. Paul insists they must remain healthy in their active, selfless love for others. Also age has not cooled their passion for Jesus.

  • Sound in Steadfastness (Patience): This is the ability to endure trials and the "long haul" of life without giving up. It is the "staying power" of a mature believer. It is endurance under pressure. The ability to handle trials and pressure well.

Maturity is not age or years in the faith, but manifest health in these areas.
