Summary

SAMARIA — (šōmĕrôn, Strong’s H8111) Meaning "Watch-mountain" or "Watch-tower."

Samaria was the capital of the Northern Kingdom of Israel, established by King Omri. After the Assyrian captivity, it became a region of syncretistic worship and ethnic tension, eventually serving as the primary cultural bridge for the Gospel’s expansion from the Jews to the Gentiles in the New Testament.

Full Study

I. The Royal Foundation: The Hill of Shemer

The city was not an ancient tribal allotment but a strategic political purchase. In 1 Kings 16:24, King Omri bought the hill from Shemer for two talents of silver. Its name, šōmĕrôn, aptly describes its geography—a fortified hill with a commanding view of the surrounding valleys. Under the Omride dynasty (specifically Ahab), it became a center of opulence, famous for its "ivory house" (1 Kings 22:39; Amos 3:15), but also the epicenter of Canaanite Baal worship.

II. The Assyrian Crisis and the "Mixed" Heritage

The definitive turning point for Samaria occurred in 722 BC. After a three-year siege, the Assyrians captured the city (2 Kings 17).

  • The Deportation: The "ten lost tribes" were carried away.

  • The Importation: The King of Assyria brought people from Babylon, Cuthah, Ava, Hamath, and Sepharvaim to settle in the cities of Samaria.

  • The Religious Synthesis: These settlers feared the Lord but also served their own gods (2 Kings 17:33). This "half-and-half" spirituality created the deep-seated Jewish prejudice that Samaritans were not "true" Israelites, but rather Kuthim (men from Cuthah).

III. The Post-Exilic Breach

When the Jews returned from Babylonian exile to rebuild the Temple in Jerusalem, the Samaritans offered to help. Zerubbabel and the Jewish leaders refused (Ezra 4:1-3), asserting that the Samaritans had no portion in Israel. This rejection led to centuries of hostility, including the construction of the rival Samaritan temple on Mount Gerizim (approx. 400 BC), which the Hasmonean king John Hyrcanus eventually destroyed in 128 BC, further embittering the two groups.

IV. The Christological Reclamation: John 4

By the time of Jesus, the animosity was so high that many Jews traveled extra miles to avoid Samaria. Jesus’ encounter at Jacob’s Well breaks three major barriers:

  1. The Ethnic Barrier: He engages a "half-breed" Samaritan.

  2. The Gender Barrier: He speaks publicly with a woman.

  3. The Liturgical Barrier: When she asks about the "place" of worship (Gerizim vs. Jerusalem), Jesus declares that the place is irrelevant. God is seeking those who worship in Spirit and Truth.

V. The Missional Pivot: Acts 1 and 8

Samaria is the vital link in the Great Commission. In Acts 1:8, Jesus specifically names Samaria as the second stage of expansion.

  • The Outpouring: In Acts 8, Philip the Evangelist goes to the city of Samaria. The response is overwhelming.

  • The Integration: When Peter and John (the "Sons of Thunder" who once wanted to incinerate a Samaritan village in Luke 9:54) arrive to pray for them, the gift of the Holy Spirit proves that the "outsiders" are now full citizens of the Kingdom.
