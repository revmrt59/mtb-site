BISHOP — (episkopos, StrongΓÇÖs G1984)
Refers to one who has the responsibility of "looking over" or guarding a group. While "Elder" (presbyteros) focuses on the leaderΓÇÖs mature character, "Bishop" focuses on his functional duty to watch over the spiritual health and protection of the congregation. Blue Letter Bible

Full Study:

1. Greek Roots / Derivation
Derived from epi (upon/over) + skopos (a watcher/lookout). It literally means "one who watches over" or "a supervisor." It is the root of the English word "Episcopal." Bible Hub

2. Meaning / Lexical Definition
An overseer, curator, or guardian. In the New Testament, it describes the office of leadership tasked with the management, protection, and feeding of the local church. It signifies a person who provides "vigilant care" for the souls entrusted to him. ThayerΓÇÖs Greek Lexicon

3. Usage in Greek Culture & Word Pictures

  • The City Superintendent: In secular Greek life, episkopos was an official administrative title for a city manager or an officer sent to a newly conquered city to organize its government and ensure its laws were followed.

  • The Lookout on the Wall: A word picture associated with episkopos is a watchman standing on a high tower. His job is to look over the landscape to spot approaching enemies (false teachers) and sound the alarm to protect the city. Precept Austin

  • The Guardian of a Minor: It was used for a tutor or guardian appointed to protect an orphan's inheritance. The Bishop "looks over" the churchΓÇÖs "inheritance" (the Gospel) to ensure it isn't stolen or corrupted.

4. Theological Implications
Jesus is the ultimate "Overseer (episkopos) of your souls" (1 Peter 2:25). Those appointed to this office are "under-shepherds" who must provide the same vigilant, sacrificial care that Christ provides. The term emphasizes accountability; an overseer must answer to the Master for how he managed the "household" (v. 7). BibleRef

5. Contextual Nuance in Titus 1:7
The NKJV says: "For a bishop (episkopos) must be blameless, as a steward of God..." Paul uses episkopos and presbyteros (v. 5) interchangeably, proving they are the same office. He shifts to episkopos here to highlight the responsibility involved. In a culture of "evil beasts" (v. 12), the church needed "Overseers" who would not sleep on the watch but would actively guard the flock from the "circumcision group" (v. 10). Enduring Word

6. Key Uses in Other Verses (NKJV)

  • Acts 20:28: "...the Holy Spirit has made you overseers (episkopos), to shepherd the church of God..." NKJV

  • 1 Timothy 3:1: "If a man desires the position of a bishop (episkopos), he desires a good work."

  • Philippians 1:1: "...to all the saints in Christ Jesus... with the bishops (episkopos) and deacons."

7. Other Relevant Insights
The distinction is crucial for leadership:

  • Elder (Presbyteros): The Dignity of the man (Maturity).

  • Bishop (Episkopos): The Duty of the man (Ministry).
    Paul demands that the "Overseer" be "blameless" because he is a "steward." You cannot "look over" others if your own life is "overlooked" or in shambles. Precept Austin
