BEAST — (th─ôrion, StrongΓÇÖs G2342)
Refers to a wild, untamed, or predatory animal. When used metaphorically in Scripture, it describes human behavior that is completely carnal, violent, and devoid of civilization, reason, or divine influence.

Full Study:

1. Greek Roots / Derivation
A diminutive of th─ôr (a wild animal). It's the common term for "beast," "wild animal," or "venomous creature." Bible Hub

2. Meaning / Lexical Definition
A wild beast; a beast of prey. In a moral sense, it means a bestial man—one who is savage, ravenous, and cruel. Thayer's Greek Lexicon

3. Usage in Greek Culture & Word Pictures

  • The GladiatorΓÇÖs Arena: This word was constantly used in Roman/Greek arenas to refer to the wild animals (lions, bears, etc.) that gladiators fought or that criminals were thrown to. It evokes images of violence and death.

  • The Unruly Child: In AristotleΓÇÖs writings on ethics, he sometimes used the concept of th─ôrion to describe a human being who was completely driven by instinct, totally lacking reason, education, or self-control—a savage.

  • The Poisonous Snake: In Acts 28:4, Paul is bitten by a th─ôrion on Malta—a venomous snake. This provides a word picture of the false teachers in Titus: their influence is venomous and lethal. Precept Austin

4. Theological Implications
The Bible teaches that humans are made in the image of God. To be called a "beast" is to have rejected that divine image and returned to a purely carnal existence governed by appetite, self-preservation, and aggression. Paul uses this word to de-humanize their spiritual state, emphasizing that their behavior is sub-human.

5. Contextual Nuance in Titus 1:12
Paul quotes the Cretan poet Epimenides who said, "Cretans are always liars, evil beasts..." The term serves as the final, most damning descriptor of the false teachers. They are not just wrong in their theology; their character is animalistic, violent, and untamed by the Holy Spirit. This harsh language justifies PaulΓÇÖs command in verse 13 to "rebuke them sharply," just as one must deal forcefully with a wild animal.

6. Key Uses in Other Verses (NKJV)

  • Acts 28:4: "When the natives saw the creature (th─ôrion) hanging from his hand..."

  • Revelation (many uses, e.g., Rev 13:1): "Then I stood on the sand of the sea. And I saw a beast (th─ôrion) rising up out of the sea..." (Used metaphorically for antichrist and evil empires).

  • 1 Corinthians 15:32: "...if after the manner of men I have fought with beasts (th─ôrion) at Ephesus..."

7. Other Relevant Insights
The "beastly" nature is linked directly to the "lazy glutton" nature. A beast consumes resources (gast─ôr), does no productive work (argos), and acts purely on instinct rather than reason (th─ôrion). This trilogy of vices paints a complete picture of a spiritually corrupted person.
