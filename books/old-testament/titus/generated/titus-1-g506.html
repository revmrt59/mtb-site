INSUBORDINATE — (anupotaktos, StrongΓÇÖs G506)
Refers to a settled refusal to submit to rightful authority. In Titus 1:6, it describes children who are unruly, lawless, or "not placed under" the government of their father, indicating a breakdown in the domestic order required for church leadership. Blue Letter Bible

Full Study:

1. Greek Roots / Derivation
A compound word from a (negative) + hupotass┼ì (to place under). It literally means "not placed under" or "unarranged." Bible Hub

2. Meaning / Lexical Definition
Unsubmissive, rebellious, or lawless. It describes an attitude that resists being governed or organized. In a New Testament context, it refers to a heart that refuses to stay in its assigned position or rank. ThayerΓÇÖs Greek Lexicon

3. Usage in Greek Culture & Word Pictures

  • The Breaking of Ranks: In secular Greek, this was a military term for a soldier who abandoned his post or refused to follow the commander's battle arrangement. The word picture is of a hole in the phalanx caused by one man's refusal to stay "under" (hupo) orders. Precept Austin

  • The Unbroken Colt: It was used in agricultural life for an ox or horse that refused the yoke. It is "unruly" and "wild," making it useless for work because it will not follow the farmer's lead.

  • The Draft Dodger: In legal records, it described those who avoided their civic duties or refused to register for the draft. They were "out of the system" and refused to acknowledge the city's authority.

4. Theological Implications
God is a God of order, and the church is His "army" and "household." Anupotaktos is the spiritual state of being un-yoked from GodΓÇÖs appointed order. If a father cannot bring his own children into a state of submission, he has failed the "management test" of the home and cannot be trusted to maintain the "rank and file" of the church. BibleRef

5. Contextual Nuance in Titus 1:6
The NKJV says: "...not accused of dissipation or insubordination (anupotaktos)." Paul identifies this as the direct opposite of the "faithful" children mentioned earlier. In Crete, where the false teachers were themselves "insubordinate" (v. 10), the elderΓÇÖs children had to be a living contrast. Their submission proved that the fatherΓÇÖs authority was healthy, effective, and rooted in the Gospel. Enduring Word

7. Other Relevant Insights
There is a profound progression here: as┼ìtia (dissipation) is the action of a wild life, while anupotaktos (insubordination) is the attitude that causes it. A child who is "insubordinate" refuses to be "placed under" the father's wisdom, which inevitably leads to the "waste" of their life. The elder must be a man who can cultivate a spirit of willing submission in his home. Precept Austin
