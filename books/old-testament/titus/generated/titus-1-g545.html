DISOBEDIENT — (apeith─ôs, StrongΓÇÖs G545)
Refers to a willful, obstinate refusal to be persuaded by the truth. It describes a heart that is intentionally "un-persuadable," resulting in an active defiance of GodΓÇÖs commands and a rejection of His authority.

Full Study:

1. Greek Roots / Derivation
Derived from a- (negative) + peith┼ì (to persuade). It literally means "one who refuses to be persuaded." Bible Hub

2. Meaning / Lexical Definition
Unbelieving, disobedient, or contumacious. In the New Testament, it goes beyond simple failure to obey; it implies a settled state of mind that has decided to reject GodΓÇÖs Word regardless of the evidence presented. ThayerΓÇÖs Greek Lexicon

3. Usage in Greek Culture & Word Pictures

  • The Obstinate Debater: In Greek philosophy and law, apeith─ôs described someone who, after hearing a clear and logical argument, refused to concede. It was the mark of a closed mind that prioritized personal opinion over proven fact.

  • The Unruly Child: It was commonly used to describe a rebellious child who hears their parentΓÇÖs instruction but chooses to "shut their ears" and do the opposite. Precept Austin

  • The Muted Compass: A word picture is that of a navigator who ignores the stars. Even when the light of truth (the stars) is clearly visible, the apeith─ôs person insists on steering the ship according to their own "fables" and "myths."

4. Theological Implications
Biblical "faith" (pistis) is rooted in being "persuaded" (peith┼ì) by God. Therefore, apeith─ôs is the opposite of faith. It is the spiritual condition of those who see the "light of the knowledge of the glory of God" but choose to "turn from the truth" (v. 14). Because they are "un-persuadable" by the Gospel, they are left with nothing but their own "commandments of men."

5. Contextual Nuance in Titus 1:16
The NKJV says: "being abominable, disobedient (apeith─ôs), and disqualified..." This is PaulΓÇÖs second verdict. The false teachers were not just "uninformed"; they were willfully defiant. Despite their "profession" to know God, they were "un-persuaded" by His character and His laws. Their disobedience was the natural fruit of a heart that refused to submit to the "sound doctrine" of Titus 1:9. BibleRef

6. Key Uses in Other Verses (NKJV)

  • Luke 1:17: "...to turn the hearts of the fathers to the children, and the disobedient (apeith─ôs) to the wisdom of the just..."

  • Romans 1:30: "...inventors of evil things, disobedient (apeith─ôs) to parents..."

  • 2 Timothy 3:2: "...proud, blasphemers, disobedient (apeith─ôs) to parents, unthankful, unholy..."

7. Other Relevant Insights
There is a progression in Titus 1:16. Because they were "abominable" (foul in nature), they became "disobedient" (rebellious in action). When a person is apeith─ôs, they are beyond the reach of gentle correction; they require the "sharp rebuke" (v. 13) to crack the shell of their "un-persuadable" heart. Bible Hub
