GREEDY ΓÇö (aischrokerd─ôs, StrongΓÇÖs G150)
Refers to a person who is eager for base or shameful gain. In Titus 1:7, it describes a man whose primary motivation is financial profit, making him unfit for stewardship because he would be willing to compromise the truth for the sake of "filthy lucre." Blue Letter Bible┬á

1. Greek Roots / Derivation
A compound word from aischros (shameful, deformed, or base) and kerdos (gain or profit). It literally means "shameful-gain-seeker." Bible Hub┬á

2. Meaning / Lexical Definition
Eager for base gain; sordidly greedy. It refers to someone who is preoccupied with personal profit to the point that they would obtain it through unseemly or dishonest methods. In the New Testament, it is the opposite of being a generous "steward of God." ThayerΓÇÖs Greek Lexicon┬á

3. Usage in Greek Culture & Word Pictures┬á

  • The Weighted Scale: In the Greek marketplace, this word described a merchant who used deceptive weights to cheat customers. The profit was "shameful" because it was stolen under the guise of trade. Precept Austin

  • The Religious Con-Artist: It was used for itinerant priests or philosophers who performed rituals solely to extract money from the superstitious. They were "greedy" for the coins, not the truth.

  • The Deformed Reward: The root aischros refers to something physically ugly or deformed. The word picture is of a man clutching gold that is covered in filthΓÇöthe money itself has become "ugly" because of the "shameful" way it was earned.┬á

4. Theological Implications
"No one can serve two masters" (Matthew 6:24). If a leader is aischrokerd─ôs, his "god" is his belly (Philippians 3:19). A steward must manage the MasterΓÇÖs resources for the MasterΓÇÖs glory; a greedy man manages them for his own pocket. This vice is the hallmark of a false teacher, who views "godliness as a means of gain" (1 Timothy 6:5). Enduring Word┬á

5. Contextual Nuance in Titus 1:7
The NKJV says: "...not violent, not greedy (aischrokerd─ôs) for money..." Paul is contrasting the elder with the false teachers in verse 11 who were "teaching things they ought not, for the sake of dishonest gain." In Crete, where "dishonesty" was a cultural trait, a leaderΓÇÖs financial integrity was his most powerful testimony. If he was greedy, he could be "bought" by the very people he was supposed to rebuke. BibleRef┬á

7. Other Relevant Insights
Greed is a form of idolatry (Colossians 3:5). The aischrokerd─ôs man is the opposite of the "Hospitable" man in verse 8. Hospitality requires spending one's resources for others; greed requires hoarding resources for oneself. A greedy man cannot "open his home" because he is too afraid of what it will cost him.
