1. Greek Roots / Derivation

2. Meaning / Lexical Definition
A deed, action, or anything accomplished by hand or art. It refers to the "fruit" of one's labor and the visible manifestation of one's character and hidden thoughts. ThayerΓÇÖs Greek Lexicon┬á

3. Usage in Greek Culture & Word Pictures┬á

  • The Finished Product: In the Greek marketplace, ergon was used for a finished piece of craftsmanshipΓÇöa pot, a statue, or a woven garment. It wasn't the "idea" of the pot, but the pot itself.

  • The Agricultural Yield: It described the work of the field and the resulting harvest. To have no "work" meant there was no grain in the barn. Precept Austin

  • The Evidence in Court: In legal settings, it was used for evidence of an act. Words could be debated, but an ergon (a deed done) was considered an undeniable fact.┬á

4. Theological Implications
While we are not saved by works (Ephesians 2:8-9), we are saved for works. Ergon is the "life-testimony" of the believer. God "will render to each one according to his deeds (ergon)" (Romans 2:6). Faith without ergon is dead (James 2:20) because it lacks the "evidence" of life.┬á

5. Contextual Nuance in Titus 1:16
The NKJV says: "but in works (ergon) they deny Him... disqualified for every good work (ergon)." Paul uses this word twice to hammer home his point: Your actions are your true language. The false teachers spoke "God-talk," but their "deeds" (greed, fables, insubordination) were shouting a denial of Christ. Because their nature was "abominable," they were "unfit" to produce any "finished product" that God could approve. BibleRef┬á

7. Other Relevant Insights
In Titus 1, "works" act as the spiritual DNA. Just as a thorn bush cannot produce grapes, a "defiled" mind cannot produce a "good work." The tragedy of the false teachers was that they were busy doing "religious things," but in God's eyes, they were doing no "good work" at all. Bible Hub┬á
