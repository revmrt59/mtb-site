Full Study:┬á

1. Greek Roots / Derivation
A compound verb from kata (down) + hist─ômi (to stand). It literally means "to set down" or "to place down permanently," implying the establishment of a fixed and recognized position. Bible Hub┬á

2. Meaning / Lexical Definition
To constitute, to designate, or to make ruler. It describes the official act of inducting a person into an office or providing them with the status required to exercise authority over others. ThayerΓÇÖs Greek Lexicon┬á

3. Usage in Greek Culture & Word Pictures┬á

  • The ShipΓÇÖs Landing: In classical Greek, it was used for bringing a ship to land or "setting it down" at its destination. The word picture is of a vessel reaching its proper station where it can finally be useful. Precept Austin

  • The Royal Steward: It was used to describe a king putting a servant in charge of his house or kingdom (e.g., Joseph in Egypt). It isn't just about "giving a job," but about "transferring authority." StudyLight

  • The Courtroom Escort: It was also used for conducting or escorting someone to a certain place or before a magistrate (Acts 17:15). It implies a "hand-off" from one authority to another. Blue Letter Bible┬á

4. Theological Implications
God is the ultimate source of all appointment. While humans (like Titus) perform the physical act, the kathist─ômi reflects GodΓÇÖs own sovereign arrangement of the church. It reminds us that leadership is not a "volunteer" position but a divine placement. Those "appointed" are stewards of GodΓÇÖs household, accountable to the Master who set them there. H.B. Charles Jr.┬á

5. Contextual Nuance in Titus 1:5
The NKJV says: "...and appoint (kathist─ômi) elders in every city..." Paul authorized Titus to take the "straightening out" (v. 5) to its logical conclusion by installing local leadership. Because the Cretan culture was "insubordinate" (v. 10), these appointments had to be permanent and official to provide the weight necessary to stop false teachers. Bible Project┬á

7. Other Relevant Insights
In the New Testament, kathist─ômi is often used in the Passive Voice regarding the High Priesthood (Hebrews 5:1, 8:3), indicating that leaders do not appoint themselves. In Titus, the authority flows from Christ to Paul, from Paul to Titus, and from Titus to the eldersΓÇöa clear chain of stewardship. Precept Austin┬á
