Full Study:┬á

1. Greek Roots / Derivation
Derived from ana- (up, or again) + trep┼ì (to turn). It literally means "to turn upside down" or "to overturn." Bible Hub┬á

2. Meaning / Lexical Definition
To subvert, to overthrow, or to render useless. In the New Testament, it is used metaphorically to describe the effect of error on a believer's lifeΓÇöit doesn't just "bother" them; it collapses their spiritual structure. Thayer's Greek Lexicon┬á

3. Usage in Greek Culture & Word Pictures┬á

  • The Overturned Table: In secular Greek, this word was used for knocking over a table or a chair. It is the same word used in the Gospels when Jesus "overturned" the tables of the money changers. It implies a sudden, violent disruption of order.

  • The Demolished Building: Architects used anatrep┼ì for the demolition of a wall or the undermining of a foundation. The building might still look standing for a moment, but its structural integrity has been destroyed from beneath. Precept Austin

  • The Capsized Ship: It was also used in nautical contexts for a ship being turned over by a wave. Once capsized, the vessel is "unfit" for its purpose and everyone on board is in peril.┬á

4. Theological Implications
Faith is often described in Scripture as a "building" or a "walk." False teaching is not just "different information"; it is a demolition crew. When the "circumcision group" taught that grace was not enough, they were "overturning" the very foundation (Christ) upon which the believers' hope was built. If the foundation is turned over, the entire Christian life collapses into legalism or despair.┬á

5. Contextual Nuance in Titus 1:11
Paul notes that they are upsetting "whole households." In the early church, the "household" (oikos) was the primary unit of the church. By infiltrating a home, these teachers were destroying the faith of the father, mother, children, and servants all at once. It shows that false doctrine is highly contagious and socially destructive.┬á
