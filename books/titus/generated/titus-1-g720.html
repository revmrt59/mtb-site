1. Greek Roots / Derivation
A primary verb (arneomai) signifying to say "no," to refuse, or to renounce. It is the direct opposite of homologe┼ì (to confess/agree). Bible Hub┬á

2. Meaning / Lexical Definition
To deny, disown, or abjure. It means to act in a way that is inconsistent with a previous claim or relationship. In the New Testament, it often refers to a refusal to recognize the claims of Christ or a betrayal of one's stated allegiance. ThayerΓÇÖs Greek Lexicon┬á

3. Usage in Greek Culture & Word Pictures┬á

  • The Disowned Child: In Roman and Greek law, it was used for disinheriting a family member. To "deny" a son meant to legally declare he no longer belonged to the household. The false teachersΓÇÖ works "legally" declared they did not belong to God.

  • The Contract Breaker: It was used for repudiating a treaty or a debt. One party simply "denied" that the agreement existed. Precept Austin

  • The Silent Contradiction: A word picture is that of a witness who remains silent or points in the opposite direction when asked for the truth. While the mouth says "I know Him," the hands and feet "deny" Him by moving toward sin.┬á

4. Theological Implications
Denial is not always verbal. While Peter denied Jesus with his tongue, the false teachers in Titus denied Him with their behavior. Scriptural "knowing" of God implies intimacy and obedience; therefore, to live in "insubordinate" (v. 10) rebellion is to "deny" the very nature of the God one claims to serve.┬á

5. Contextual Nuance in Titus 1:16
The NKJV says: "They profess to know God, but in works they deny (arneomai) Him." Paul is pointing out a "double-life." Their "works" (their "beastly" behavior, greed, and fables) were the real testimony. By their actions, they were telling the world that God is not holy, not authoritative, and not enough. Their lifestyle was a constant renunciation of the Gospel. BibleRef┬á
