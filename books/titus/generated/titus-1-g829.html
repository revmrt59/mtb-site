1. Greek Roots / Derivation
A compound word from autos (self) + h─ôdomai (to please or be gratified). It literally means "self-pleasing." It describes someone who determines their own path without regard for others. Bible Hub┬á

2. Meaning / Lexical Definition
Self-willed, headstrong, or obstinate. It signifies an arrogant person who, in their overweening self-confidence, maintains their own opinion and asserts their own rights while being insensitive to the feelings or needs of others. ThayerΓÇÖs Greek Lexicon┬á

3. Usage in Greek Culture & Word Pictures┬á

  • The Tyrannical Ruler: In secular Greek, authad─ôs was used for despots and tyrants who governed by whim rather than law. They "pleased themselves" at the expense of the people. Precept Austin

  • The Unbending Branch: A word picture associated with it is a stiff, dry branch that refuses to bend in the wind and eventually snaps. It lacks the "flexibility" of grace and humility.

  • The Solo Navigator: It evokes the image of a captain who throws away the charts and ignores the lighthouse, insisting on steering the ship solely by his own "gut feeling."┬á

4. Theological Implications
Self-will is the very essence of sin; it is the "I will" of Lucifer (Isaiah 14:13). A leader in the church must be "God-willed," not "self-willed." If a man is authad─ôs, he cannot be a "steward" (v. 7) because a stewardΓÇÖs only job is to carry out the MasterΓÇÖs will. A self-willed man eventually turns the church into a monument to himself rather than a temple for God. BibleRef┬á

5. Contextual Nuance in Titus 1:7
The NKJV says: "...as a steward of God, not self-willed (authad─ôs)..." Paul is warning Titus against the "Cretan temper." In a culture of "beasts" (v. 12), everyone was "self-pleasing." An elder must be the opposite: a man of meekness and submission. If he is self-willed, he will be "quick-tempered" (the next vice) whenever his "self" is challenged. Enduring Word┬á
