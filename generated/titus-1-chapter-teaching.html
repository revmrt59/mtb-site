TITUS 1: LEADERSHIP THAT SHAPES A HEALTHY CHURCH (NKJV)

Purpose of This Chapter
Titus 1 explains how God establishes order and health in His church. Paul shows that truth which produces godliness, qualified leadership appointed according to God’s design, and the courage to confront false teaching are essential for the protection and maturity of God’s people.

Chapter Outline

  1. Paul establishes Titus’ authority in Crete (Titus 1:1–4)

  2. Why Titus was left in Crete and what was lacking (Titus 1:5)

  3. God’s design for church leadership: qualified elders (Titus 1:6–9)

  4. The danger of false teachers and the need for correction (Titus 1:10–16)

Paul establishes Titus’ authority in Crete (Titus 1:1–4)

Titus 1:1–4 Paul, a bondservant of God and an apostle of Jesus Christ, according to the faith of God’s elect and the acknowledgment of the truth which accords with godliness, in hope of eternal life which God, who cannot lie, promised before time began, but has in due time manifested His word through preaching, which was committed to me according to the commandment of God our Savior; To Titus, a true son in our common faith: Grace, mercy, and peace from God the Father and the Lord Jesus Christ our Savior. (NKJV)

Paul begins the letter by establishing authority, not for personal status, but for stewardship. He identifies himself first as a servant belonging to God and then as one sent by Jesus Christ. Authority is grounded in calling and faithfulness, not personality or position.

Paul defines truth by its outcome. Truth that comes from God produces godliness. Teaching that does not lead to a changed life has failed its purpose. Paul anchors this truth in the hope of eternal life, promised by God before time began and revealed through preaching at the proper time. The message is trustworthy because it rests on the character of God, who cannot lie.

Titus is addressed as a true son in a common faith. This language emphasizes shared belief and shared life rather than hierarchy. Paul’s confidence in Titus is relational and spiritual, not merely administrative.

Why Titus was left in Crete and what was lacking (Titus 1:5)

Titus 1:5 For this reason I left you in Crete, that you should set in order the things that are lacking, and appoint elders in every city as I commanded you. (NKJV)

Paul states plainly why Titus was left in Crete. Churches existed, but they were incomplete and disordered. What was lacking was not enthusiasm or activity, but structure, qualified leadership, and protection from harmful influences.

Paul addresses leadership first because leadership shapes doctrine, direction, culture, health, and protection. Setting things in order means bringing the life of the church into alignment with what God has already revealed. Disorder left unaddressed spreads and harms people, especially households.

God’s design for church leadership: qualified elders (Titus 1:6–9)

Titus 1:6–9 If a man is blameless, the husband of one wife, having faithful children not accused of dissipation or insubordination. For a bishop must be blameless, as a steward of God, not self-willed, not quick-tempered, not given to wine, not violent, not greedy for money, but hospitable, a lover of what is good, sober-minded, just, holy, self-controlled, holding fast the faithful word as he has been taught, that he may be able, by sound doctrine, both to exhort and convict those who contradict. (NKJV)

Paul outlines qualifications for elders that focus on character, household faithfulness, self-control, relational maturity, and doctrinal stability. Leadership in the church is stewardship. Elders manage what belongs to God and must therefore live in a way that is credible and trustworthy.

These qualifications show that leadership is tested over time. The home is the proving ground for spiritual maturity. The ability to teach and correct flows from holding firmly to the faithful word. Authority is exercised through faithfulness to Scripture, not force of personality.

Each church is to be locally governed by a plurality of qualified elders. Shared oversight provides accountability, balance, and protection for the body.

The danger of false teachers and the need for correction (Titus 1:10–16)

Titus 1:10–16 For there are many insubordinate, both idle talkers and deceivers, especially those of the circumcision, whose mouths must be stopped, who subvert whole households, teaching things which they ought not, for the sake of dishonest gain… They profess to know God, but in works they deny Him, being abominable, disobedient, and disqualified for every good work. (NKJV)

Paul explains why qualified leadership is necessary. False teachers are not harmless. Their words undermine faith and destabilize households. In a difficult cultural environment, these voices must be confronted directly.

Correction is commanded so that people may become sound in the faith. The goal is restoration and health, not humiliation. Paul concludes with a sober diagnosis. Some profess to know God, but their lives deny Him. Obedience, not verbal profession, is the evidence of genuine knowledge of God.

This closing warning prepares the way for chapters 2 and 3, where sound doctrine is displayed through everyday life and good works.
